Genetic Control of Flowering Time in Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.) and Related Arachis Species

1. Introduction to Flowering Time Control in Peanut (Arachis hypogaea)

Flowering time is a paramount agronomic trait in cultivated peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.), serving as a critical determinant for the adaptation of distinct varieties to specific agroecological environments. This trait directly influences the duration of the vegetative growth phase, the window available for pod development, and consequently, the ultimate yield potential. Deviations from optimal flowering periods, whether too early or too late, can result in diminished yields. The transition from vegetative to reproductive growth, known as the floral transition, represents a pivotal stage in the lifecycle of crop plants and is meticulously governed by the timing of flowering. A comprehensive understanding of the regulatory mechanisms underpinning flowering time is therefore indispensable for breeding programs, particularly those aiming to develop early-maturing cultivars. Such cultivars are highly desirable in regions characterized by short growing seasons or those prone to terminal drought stress, where an accelerated lifecycle can mitigate yield losses.1

Peanut possesses a unique reproductive biology, often described as "blossoming on the ground and bearing pods underground". This phenomenon, termed geocarpy, involves aerial flowering followed by the elongation of a specialized structure, the gynophore (or peg), which carries the fertilized ovules into the soil for pod development.2 This unusual developmental strategy necessitates a precise coordination between the timing of flowering, environmental conditions, and the subsequent geocarpic development processes.3 The success of underground pod formation is contingent upon the gynophore reaching and penetrating the soil, implying that flowering must occur at a juncture that permits adequate peg elongation and soil entry under favorable conditions. This intricate link suggests that genes controlling flowering might be co-regulated with, or exert pleiotropic effects on, genes governing peg development or the light and dark sensory mechanisms essential for subterranean pod maturation. Such a layer of regulatory complexity is not commonly observed in aerial-fruiting crops and indicates that selection pressures on flowering time genes in peanut may be uniquely intertwined with its geocarpic habit. Consequently, breeding efforts targeting flowering time could inadvertently influence linked traits crucial for successful underground fruiting.

Similar to other plant species, flowering time in peanut is recognized as a complex quantitative trait. Its expression is orchestrated by the intricate interplay of multiple genes, which in turn interact dynamically with various environmental cues, most notably photoperiod and temperature.5 The allotetraploid nature of cultivated peanut (Arachis hypogaea; AABB genomes, 2n=4x=40), which arose from a hybridization event between the diploid progenitors Arachis duranensis (A genome) and Arachis ipaensis (B genome) 6, introduces an additional dimension of genetic complexity. This polyploid origin results in the presence of homoeologous gene pairs from the A and B subgenomes for many traits, including those involved in flowering time regulation. Studies on various gene families, such as Phosphatidylethanolamine-Binding Proteins (PEBPs) 7, WRKY transcription factors 5, R2R3-MYB transcription factors 8, and IGT (I ACT IN GRAVITROPISM AND TILLER ANGLE CONTROL 1) genes 9, have indicated instances of subgenome dominance or differential expression patterns between homoeologs. This genetic redundancy can provide a buffering capacity against deleterious mutations but also furnishes the raw material for evolutionary divergence through processes like subfunctionalization (partitioning of ancestral functions) or neofunctionalization (acquisition of novel functions). Such evolutionary trajectories could lead to more nuanced and sophisticated control of flowering time, enabling adaptation to a diverse array of environmental conditions. Discerning the relative contributions of alleles from each subgenome is therefore a key consideration for developing targeted and effective breeding strategies in peanut.

2. Major Gene Families and Key Regulatory Proteins Implicated in Peanut Flowering Time

The regulation of flowering time in peanut involves a diverse array of gene families and regulatory proteins, many of which are conserved across the plant kingdom. Research in Arachis species has begun to elucidate the specific members of these families and their roles in orchestrating the floral transition.

2.1. CONSTANS-like (COL) Gene Family

The CONSTANS-like (COL) gene family plays a central role in the photoperiod pathway, enabling plants to perceive and respond to changes in day length to initiate flowering. In Arachis hypogaea, a comprehensive analysis has identified 18 AhCOL genes. These genes are characterized by the presence of conserved B-box zinc finger and CCT (CONSTANS, CO-like, and TOC1) domains, and all are predicted to localize to the nucleus.10 The specific gene identifiers (e.g., arahy.6J3MKV.1 for AhCOL1) and their respective chromosomal locations have been documented (Table 1, derived from). Expression profiling studies have revealed that the majority of AhCOL genes are abundantly expressed in leaves, flowers, and, interestingly, in fruit needles (developing gynophores), suggesting roles beyond floral initiation.3

Crucially, protein-protein interaction studies have begun to map the connectivity of AhCOL proteins within the regulatory network. AhCOL1 has been shown to interact with AhNF-YC1 (Nuclear Factor Y, subunit C1), while AhCOL3 interacts with both AhNF-YC1 and AhCOP1 (CONSTITUTIVE PHOTOMORPHOGENESIS 1).10 These interactions are highly significant as NF-YC is a component of the NF-Y transcription factor complex, which, in conjunction with CO/COL proteins, directly binds to the promoter of FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) to activate its expression in model systems like Arabidopsis. Conversely, COP1, an E3 ubiquitin ligase, targets CO/COL proteins for proteasomal degradation, particularly in darkness, thereby acting as a repressor of flowering. The observed interactions in peanut strongly suggest the conservation of this core regulatory module (CO/COL-NF-YC activation of FT, and COP1-mediated degradation of CO/COL), linking the photoperiod sensing machinery to the downstream activation of florigen signals. This implies that strategies aimed at manipulating these specific protein interactions could offer novel avenues for modulating flowering time in peanut.

Furthermore, certain AhCOL genes, including AhCOL4, AhCOL8, AhCOL13, and AhCOL14, exhibit upregulation in response to light induction and mechanical stress.10 While pod development is a post-flowering process, its initiation is intrinsically linked to flowering signals. The responsiveness of these AhCOL genes to mechanical stress is particularly intriguing in the context of peanut's geocarpic reproductive strategy. The penetration of the gynophore into the soil is inherently a mechanical process. If these AhCOL genes, which are potentially involved in light sensing and flowering regulation, are also sensitive to mechanical cues during pegging and subsequent pod development, it suggests a dual functionality or a continued involvement of photoperiod pathway components in the regulation of subterranean fruit development. This could represent an adaptive mechanism for coordinating the unique underground fruiting habit of peanut with environmental signals.

2.2. Phosphatidylethanolamine-Binding Protein (PEBP) Family (FT, TFL1, MFT-like)

The Phosphatidylethanolamine-Binding Protein (PEBP) family encompasses critical regulators of the floral transition and plant architecture. Within this family, FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT)-like proteins are generally recognized as mobile floral promoting signals (florigen), while TERMINAL FLOWER 1 (TFL1)-like proteins typically act as floral repressors and play roles in maintaining indeterminate inflorescence meristems. MOTHER OF FT AND TFL1 (MFT)-like proteins are considered to represent an ancestral lineage within the PEBP family, often involved in processes such as seed development and germination.

In cultivated peanut, 33 PEBP genes have been identified and categorized into FT, TFL, MFT, and FT-like subgroups.7 Evidence suggests that some orthologous PEBP genes exhibit subgenome dominance, reflecting the allotetraploid nature of A. hypogaea.7 Several key AhPEBP genes have been implicated in controlling the flowering response to different genotypes, photoperiods, and temperatures. These include ArahyM2THPA, ArahyEM6VH3, Arahy4GAQ4U, and ArahyIZ8FG5, which are classified as FT-like and are proposed to promote flowering.7 Conversely, ArahyG6F3P2 and ArahyLUT2QN, also FT-like, have been suggested to inhibit flowering based on expression patterns.11 Within the TFL-like group, ArahyDYRS20 and ArahyBBG51B have been identified as key genes, and intriguingly, their elevated expression is associated with the promotion of flowering.11 This promoting role is atypical for TFL1 homologs, which are canonical repressors, and suggests a potential functional divergence in peanut. The gene Arahy.UVRI66, annotated as "flowering locus protein T," is also listed in PeanutBase expression datasets.12 The specific locus identifiers for the eight key PEBP genes mentioned above are expected to be found in Supplementary Table S1 of the study by Zhang et al. (2022, PMC9604132).11

The significant impact of TFL1-like genes in legumes is further highlighted by studies in common bean, where a TFL1 homolog (Phvul.001G189200) was found to exert major effects on flowering time, vegetative growth habit, and overall plant production. This underscores the conserved importance of this PEBP subfamily member in controlling key developmental transitions in related species.

The existence of FT-like genes in peanut whose expression appears to inhibit flowering (e.g., ArahyG6F3P2, ArahyLUT2QN 11) is a noteworthy finding. While FT proteins are typically potent floral activators, this observation points towards a more sophisticated regulatory system in peanut. It may represent instances of neofunctionalization or subfunctionalization within the FT clade, allowing for a more nuanced modulation of the photoperiodic response across diverse environmental conditions or developmental stages than would be possible with a simple binary (on/off) FT signal. This could involve competitive interactions among different FT-like paralogs for downstream partners or differential targeting to regulatory pathways.

Similarly, the atypical flowering-promoting role ascribed to the TFL-like genes ArahyDYRS20 and ArahyBBG51B 11 represents a significant deviation from the well-established repressive function of TFL1 in Arabidopsis and other species. This functional shift, if confirmed, warrants detailed investigation into the specific protein domains of these peanut TFL-like proteins or their interacting partners. It is possible that structural alterations have modified their binding affinities or downstream effects, or they may represent a distinct lineage of PEBPs with a derived, specialized function in peanut. This highlights the critical importance of experimental validation of gene function in the target species, rather than relying solely on homology-based inferences from model plants.

2.3. WRKY Transcription Factors

WRKY transcription factors constitute a large family in plants, involved in a multitude of physiological processes including growth, development, and responses to biotic and abiotic stresses.13 Their involvement in the regulation of flowering time is an area of growing research interest.5 In Arachis species, including A. duranensis, A. ipaensis, A. monticola, and the cultivated A. hypogaea, WRKY TFs have been shown to modulate flowering time through their participation in diverse regulatory pathways. These include the aging, autonomous, circadian clock, hormone, photoperiod, sugar, temperature, and vernalization pathways.5

Protein interaction network analyses have predicted that these WRKY TFs interact with key flowering regulatory proteins such as FRUITFULL (FUL), a MADS-box transcription factor, and APETALA2 (AP2), a floral repressor and developmental regulator.14 This suggests that WRKY TFs may act as crucial nodes integrating signals from various pathways to fine-tune the flowering response. For instance, the interaction with FUL (a downstream target of FT) and AP2 (a floral repressor) could allow WRKYs to modulate the balance between floral promotion and repression, or to mediate crosstalk between the photoperiod/FT pathway and developmental programs. The observation that flowering-implicated WRKY TFs often exhibit low expression levels, while their interacting partners show varied expression patterns, might indicate a stoichiometric sensitivity in these interactions or a role for WRKYs as signaling hubs rather than as bulk transcriptional effectors.14

Further evidence for their role comes from homology studies; 16 WRKY TFs identified in Arachis are homologs of the Arabidopsis genes AtWRKY12 and AtWRKY75, which are known to participate in flowering time control.5 Specific homoeologous gene pairs in A. hypogaea, such as AhTWRKY35/AhTWRKY116 and AhTWRKY69/AhTWRKY149, have been implicated in regulating flowering time.5 Additionally, a genome-wide association study (GWAS) identified a WRKY TF-encoding gene as one of seven candidate genes significantly associated with flowering time in a diverse panel of peanut germplasm.15 PeanutBase expression data also lists Aradu.S8SMF and Arahy.RFW3PD with annotations as WRKY transcription factors.12

An interesting evolutionary aspect is the reported asymmetric evolutionary pattern of WRKY TF binding sites within the promoters of homologous flowering-time genes across different Arachis species. Changes in cis-regulatory elements, such as TF binding sites, are a primary mechanism for driving evolutionary changes in gene expression patterns without altering the protein sequence itself. If these binding sites are evolving divergently in different Arachis lineages, it implies that the same WRKY TFs might regulate their target genes with different efficiencies, timing, or in response to different cues in, for example, A. duranensis compared to A. hypogaea. This molecular evolution at the cis-regulatory level could underpin some of the observed variations in flowering behavior and environmental adaptability among wild and cultivated peanut species.

2.4. MYB Transcription Factors (especially R2R3-MYB)

The R2R3-MYB transcription factors represent one of the largest and most diverse transcription factor families in plants, implicated in a wide array of biological processes, including plant development, primary and secondary metabolism, and responses to environmental stresses.8 In Arachis hypogaea, a genome-wide analysis has identified 196 typical R2R3-MYB genes, designated AhMYB001 through AhMYB196.8

Several AhMYB genes have been linked to traits related to flowering or subsequent reproductive development. For instance, AhMYB51 (potentially corresponding to locus Arahy.04JNDX) has emerged as a strong candidate gene underlying quantitative trait loci (QTLs) on chromosome A09 associated with the rate of multiple pods per plant (RMSP), a key yield component influenced by flowering characteristics.18 AhMYB51 is hypothesized to influence RMSP by affecting brassinosteroid metabolism or signaling within the flowers.18 This suggests a connection where hormonal regulation within the floral structures, mediated by an MYB factor, can impact the success of flower development into pods, thereby linking flowering quality to yield.

Another R2R3-MYB gene, AhMYB033 (orthologous to AdMYB03-18 from A. duranensis), has been associated with variations in overall plant architecture. A single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) within AhMYB033 was found to be significantly correlated with total branch number, pod length, and root-shoot ratio, highlighting its role in broader developmental processes that can indirectly affect reproductive success.8 While AhTc1 (also known as AhMYB099) is well-characterized for its role in regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis, leading to purple testa coloration in peanut 8, its direct involvement in flowering time control is less evident from the available data.

Phylogenetic and expression analyses provide further clues. Homologs of Arabidopsis R2R3-MYBs in subgroup S18, known to be involved in anther and pollen development, suggest similar roles for their peanut counterparts.8 Similarly, AhR2R3-MYB genes belonging to subgroup S14, whose Arabidopsis orthologs control axillary meristem development, show high expression in peanut peg tips, roots, and reproductive shoot tips, indicating potential functions in meristem maintenance and reproductive organ development.20 Furthermore, one of the seven candidate genes identified through a GWAS for flowering time in peanut encodes an R2R3-MYB transcription factor.15 PeanutBase expression data also includes Aradu.G406S and Arahy.JN4NL9, annotated as MYB transcription factors.12

The allotetraploid genome of peanut provides opportunities for functional divergence of homoeologous gene pairs. Studies have reported biased expression of R2R3-MYB homoeologs (derived from the A and B subgenomes) in specific tissues or developmental stages.8 This phenomenon indicates that polyploidization has likely facilitated the evolution of specialized roles for these duplicated MYB genes. Such partitioning of functions could contribute to the refined regulatory control necessary for peanut's complex developmental programs, including the precise timing of flowering and the unique processes involved in geocarpic fruit development. This functional diversification is a common evolutionary outcome of polyploidy and likely plays a significant role in how peanut adapts its reproductive strategies to varying environmental contexts.

2.5. Basic Helix-Loop-Helix (bHLH) Transcription Factors

Basic Helix-Loop-Helix (bHLH) transcription factors constitute another extensive family in plants, participating in a diverse spectrum of regulatory functions, including the control of flowering time as demonstrated in model species like Arabidopsis thaliana.15 In peanut, a gene encoding a bHLH transcription factor was identified as one of seven promising candidate genes emerging from a GWAS focused on flowering time.15 This finding directly implicates the bHLH family in the genetic architecture of this crucial agronomic trait in Arachis.

PeanutBase expression datasets provide further evidence of bHLH gene activity, listing Arahy.YHH73E and Arahy.N7XQZF with annotations as bHLH transcription factors.12 Numerous other Arachis gene identifiers in these datasets are also associated with bHLH domains, although their specific connection to flowering is not always explicit in the provided annotations.12

A particularly relevant subgroup of bHLH TFs are the Phytochrome Interacting Factors (PIFs). These proteins are key mediators of light signaling. Research on peanut planting densities has shown that monoseeding (single seed per planting hole) leads to decreased expression of PIF1 and PIF4.22 This altered expression was associated with modifications in shade-avoidance responses, which are known to influence plant architecture and can, in turn, affect the timing of flowering.22 The involvement of bHLH TFs like PIFs in mediating shade-avoidance responses, coupled with the identification of a bHLH gene in a flowering time GWAS, suggests a significant role for this TF family in integrating light quality signals with developmental programs in peanut. Light quality, particularly the red to far-red ratio which PIFs help perceive via phytochromes, is a critical environmental cue. This indicates that peanut's flowering is not solely dependent on photoperiod duration but also on the spectral quality of light, with bHLH TFs acting as key molecular players in this integration. This is especially pertinent for a crop that develops its fruit underground, where early canopy establishment and light interception efficiency, influenced by shade avoidance, can significantly impact the plant's overall vigor and resource allocation for reproduction.

2.6. FAR-RED IMPAIRED RESPONSE 1 (FAR1) Transcription Factors

The FAR-RED IMPAIRED RESPONSE 1 (FAR1) transcription factors and their homologs are recognized as important components of phytochrome A (phyA)-mediated far-red light signaling pathways. These TFs play crucial roles in various aspects of plant growth and development.2 A notable feature in Arachis hypogaea is the significant expansion of the AhFAR1 gene family, with 246 members identified.2 This expansion is hypothesized to be particularly relevant to peanut's unique geocarpic reproductive strategy and the development of subterranean pods.2

Evidence linking FAR1 TFs to flowering time includes the identification of a FAR1-encoding gene as one of the seven candidate genes from a GWAS for flowering time in peanut.15 While this suggests a role in floral transition, much of the research on AhFAR1 genes has focused on their post-flowering roles. For example, three specific AhFAR1 candidate genes, Ahy_A10g049543, Ahy_A06g026579, and Ahy_A10g048401, exhibit expression patterns indicating their specific involvement in peg and pod development.2 Although these are post-flowering events, the initiation and progression of peg and pod development are intrinsically linked to the signals and timing established during flowering.

The substantial expansion of the FAR1 gene family in Arachis and its strong association with pod development, combined with a GWAS linkage to flowering time, points towards an extended and diversified role for phyA-mediated light signaling in peanut's reproductive cycle. PhyA is the primary photoreceptor for far-red light. While its influence on flowering initiation is well-established in many plants, its role in peanut appears to be amplified and co-opted for critical post-flowering events, such as the directed growth of the gynophore towards dark soil environments and the subsequent development of the pod. The proliferation of FAR1 genes in peanut could provide the genetic toolkit necessary for these specialized light-responsive processes. The appearance of a FAR1 gene in a flowering time GWAS could indicate a direct influence on the floral transition itself, or it might relate to an effect on a closely coupled trait, such as the "time to pegging," which could be phenotypically assessed as part of a broader "flowering time" or "maturity" evaluation. This highlights the often-interconnected nature of flowering and early fruit development stages, particularly in species with unique reproductive strategies like geocarpy.

2.7. APETALA2/Ethylene Response Factor (AP2/ERF) Family

The APETALA2/Ethylene Response Factor (AP2/ERF) superfamily is one of the largest groups of transcription factors in plants, involved in a wide range of processes including plant growth, development (notably floral organogenesis), and responses to various biotic and abiotic stresses.23 Within this superfamily, the AP2 subfamily members are typically characterized by the presence of two AP2 DNA-binding domains. These proteins are known to play significant roles in regulating the development of floral organs, as well as in embryo and seed growth.23

In Arachis hypogaea, a genome-wide survey identified 185 AhAP2/ERF members. Among these, 59 genes were classified into the AP2 subfamily.23 The specific gene identifiers and chromosomal locations for these AhAP2 genes are detailed in the supplementary tables of the original research publication by JGJ et al. (2021, Frontiers in Genetics).23

An important regulatory connection has been established through the interaction of WRKY transcription factors with AP2 proteins, which collectively modulate flowering time in Arachis species.14 AP2 is a well-characterized floral repressor and also plays a role in defining floral organ identity. The interaction between WRKY TFs (often involved in stress signaling) and AP2 proteins suggests a sophisticated regulatory module. This connection provides a direct mechanism through which environmental stress signals, often transduced by WRKYs, can impinge upon floral developmental programs and the timing of flowering, which are partly controlled by AP2. Such a link could represent an adaptive strategy for peanut, allowing the plant to adjust its reproductive investment and timing based on prevailing environmental conditions, thereby optimizing its chances for successful reproduction.

2.8. Phytochrome (PHY) Genes

Phytochromes are a family of photoreceptors that plants use to sense red (R) and far-red (FR) light, playing indispensable roles in regulating a multitude of developmental processes. These include seed germination, de-etiolation, shade avoidance responses, and, critically, the photoperiodic control of flowering.2 In Arabidopsis, five distinct phytochrome genes (PHYA through PHYE) have been characterized.

Research in peanut has led to the identification of several phytochrome genes, including AhphyA, AhphyA-like, AhphyB, and AhphyE in cultivated peanut (Arachis hypogaea).4 The full coding sequences (CDS) for these cultivated peanut phytochromes have been obtained.4 For the diploid wild relatives, specific gene IDs such as Aradu.G6IAK (A. duranensis phyA) and Araip.K62H2 (A. ipaensis phyA) have also been reported.4

Studies on planting density in peanut have revealed differential expression of phytochrome genes; for instance, PhyA expression was observed to decrease while PhyB expression increased under monoseeding conditions compared to traditional planting patterns.22 These changes in phytochrome gene expression were linked to alterations in shade-avoidance responses.22 PhyB is the primary sensor for changes in the R:FR ratio, which signals the proximity of neighboring plants. An increase in PhyB expression (and a concomitant decrease in PhyA) under monoseeding could modify the plant's perception of competition, leading to a less pronounced shade-avoidance syndrome (e.g., reduced stem elongation, increased branching). Given that phytochrome signaling is intrinsically linked to the molecular pathways controlling flowering time (for instance, by influencing the stability of CONSTANS protein), such agronomic practice-induced alterations in phytochrome gene expression could have consequential effects on the timing of floral initiation and the overall duration of maturity in peanut.

While much of the research on peanut phytochromes has focused on their role in the unique geocarpic development of pods 4, their fundamental role in light perception implies an inherent involvement in flowering time regulation, as documented extensively in other plant species.25

2.9. GIGANTEA (GI)

GIGANTEA (GI) encodes a plant-specific nuclear protein that is a key component of the circadian clock machinery and plays a crucial role in integrating light signals for the photoperiodic control of flowering.26 GI typically acts upstream of, or in concert with, other central flowering regulators like CONSTANS (CO) and FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT).29 The expression of GI itself is under circadian clock control, exhibiting rhythmic accumulation with peaks occurring at specific times of the day, influenced by day length.26

In Arachis hypogaea, the ortholog Arahy.SYK0BG has been identified and annotated as encoding the GIGANTEA protein, as evidenced by PeanutBase expression data.12 The presence of a GIGANTEA ortholog in peanut strongly implies that the core circadian clock mechanism, which gates photoperiodic responses primarily through the GI-CO-FT regulatory module, is conserved and operative in this legume species. The intricate molecular clockwork detailed in Arabidopsis, such as GI's involvement in the light-dependent degradation of CYCLING DOF FACTORs (CDFs) which are repressors of CO transcription 27, is likely to be functionally mirrored in peanut. Consequently, natural or induced variations within the Arahy.SYK0BG gene or its regulatory regions could be significant sources of the diversity observed in flowering time and photoperiod sensitivity among different peanut cultivars.

2.10. Other Key Flowering Regulators (CO, FUL, Chlorophyll a-b binding protein)

Beyond the major families detailed above, several other proteins are integral to the flowering process.

CONSTANS (CO), and its homologs the CONSTANS-like (COL) proteins (discussed in section 2.1), are central integrators of light and circadian clock signals, acting to promote flowering primarily through the activation of FT.27

FRUITFULL (FUL) is a MADS-box transcription factor that functions downstream of FT. It is involved in establishing floral meristem identity and promoting the transition to flowering. In Arachis, WRKY transcription factors have been shown to interact with FUL, suggesting a modulation of FUL's activity or integration of other signals through this interaction.14

A Chlorophyll a-b binding protein encoding gene was identified as one of seven candidate genes from a GWAS for flowering time in peanut.15 While not a direct component of canonical flowering pathways, its association is intriguing. Such proteins are essential for light harvesting and photosynthesis. An association with flowering time could reflect the plant's energy status, as adequate photosynthetic output is often a prerequisite for the energetically demanding process of reproduction. Alternatively, chlorophyll-binding proteins or chloroplast-derived signals can sometimes participate in retrograde signaling from the chloroplast to the nucleus, influencing gene expression related to development in response to light conditions or metabolic status. This finding opens an avenue to explore the roles of metabolic signals or chloroplast-to-nucleus communication in the fine-tuning of flowering time in peanut.

3. Genomic Regions and Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs) Associated with Flowering Time and Related Traits

The identification of genomic regions harboring genes that control flowering time and related developmental traits is paramount for understanding the genetic architecture of these complex characteristics and for facilitating molecular breeding. Quantitative Trait Locus (QTL) mapping and Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) are powerful approaches extensively used in peanut genetics to pinpoint such regions.

Several significant QTLs influencing flowering time or closely associated traits have been reported in Arachis. A notable QTL located on linkage group 06 (LG06) was identified from an interspecific cross between A. duranensis (wild A-genome progenitor) and A. cardenasii (another wild diploid). This single QTL was found to explain a substantial 44% of the phenotypic variation for main stem flowering time.3 This finding underscores the rich reservoir of potentially valuable alleles for flowering time modification that resides within wild Arachis germplasm, which could be harnessed for cultivated peanut improvement. The magnitude of this QTL's effect suggests that introgression from wild relatives can be a potent strategy for achieving significant alterations in flowering characteristics.

In cultivated peanut, studies focusing on yield components that are intrinsically linked to flowering have also identified relevant QTLs. For instance, two stable, major-effect QTLs, designated qRMPA09.1 and qRMPA09.2, were detected on chromosome A09.18 These QTLs are associated with the rate of multiple pods per plant (RMSP), a trait influenced by the efficiency and characteristics of flowering. The R2R3-MYB transcription factor gene AhMYB51 has been proposed as a candidate gene residing within this genomic region, potentially influencing RMSP through its effects on brassinosteroid levels in flowers.18 These QTLs were mapped to chromosomal intervals of approximately 400 Kb and 600 Kb, respectively.18 The co-localization of a QTL for a post-flowering yield trait (RMSP) with a candidate gene (AhMYB51) implicated in floral biology highlights either pleiotropic effects of the underlying gene(s) or tight genetic linkage between genes controlling floral initiation/development and those governing subsequent fruit set. This implies that selection for flowering time might also lead to correlated responses in these downstream yield components.

GWAS approaches, leveraging natural diversity, have further expanded the inventory of genomic regions associated with flowering time. A study involving 390 diverse peanut germplasm accessions, evaluated across five different environments, identified 259 Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) significantly associated with flowering time.3 Within the confidence intervals of recurrently identified loci, seven candidate genes were predicted. These genes encode various types of proteins, including an R2R3-MYB transcription factor, a chlorophyll a-b binding protein, a bHLH transcription factor, a WRKY transcription factor, and a FAR1 transcription factor.15 These associated loci were distributed across multiple chromosomes (A01, A02, A03, A04, A05, A07, A10, B01, B02, B03, B04, B06, B07, and B09) and individually explained a relatively small proportion of the phenotypic variation, ranging from 3.47% to 8.58%.15 Specific gene identifiers for these seven candidates were not provided in the primary publication.15 The identification of numerous loci with minor effects through this large-scale GWAS confirms the highly polygenic nature of flowering time in peanut. This genetic architecture suggests that achieving substantial modifications in flowering time via breeding will likely necessitate the pyramiding of multiple favorable alleles from these minor-effect QTLs/genes, making genomic selection strategies potentially more effective than marker-assisted selection (MAS) targeting only a few major genes.

Research has also focused on "time-to-maturation" (TTM), a trait closely related to flowering time, especially in specific market types like Virginia peanuts. Two consistent QTLs for TTM were identified in a Recombinant Inbred Line (RIL) population derived from two Virginia-type cultivars. These are qMIA04a,b, located on chromosome A04 within a 6.9 Mbp region (flanked by markers AX-176819644_A04 and AX-176815499_A04), and qMIB03a,b, on chromosome B03 spanning a 1.8 Mbp region (flanked by markers AX-176807311_B03 and AX-176806413_B03).1 These QTLs accounted for 9.9–11.9% and 9.3–9.9% of the phenotypic variance for maturity index, respectively.1 Candidate genes within these specific TTM QTL regions were not explicitly detailed in the cited source.1 Additionally, other studies have reported QTLs associated with seed number, another yield component influenced by flowering, on chromosomes A05 and B06.18

4. Key Regulatory Pathways Orchestrating Flowering in Peanut

The transition from vegetative growth to flowering in plants is a complex process governed by an intricate network of interacting genetic pathways that integrate both endogenous cues and external environmental signals. Evidence gathered from research in Arachis species suggests that the core components and logic of these pathways are largely conserved, although potentially adapted to peanut's specific biology.

The Photoperiod Pathway is particularly prominent, enabling plants to synchronize their flowering with favorable seasons by sensing changes in day length. Key molecular players identified or inferred in peanut's photoperiod pathway include members of the CONSTANS (CO) / CONSTANS-like (COL) gene family, the circadian clock-associated protein GIGANTEA (GI) 12, photoreceptors like Phytochromes (PHY) 2, and the downstream mobile signal FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT). Interactions within this pathway are being uncovered in peanut, such as AhCOL1's interaction with AhNF-YC1 and AhCOL3's interaction with both AhNF-YC1 and AhCOP1, mirroring regulatory modules known from Arabidopsis. The model derived from Arabidopsis suggests GI interacts with FLAVIN-BINDING, KELCH REPEAT, F BOX protein 1 (FKF1) to mediate the degradation of CO repressors (CDFs), thus allowing CO accumulation and FT activation under inductive long days.27 The presence of GI and COL homologs in peanut points to the likely operation of this mechanism.

Beyond photoperiod, several other pathways contribute significantly to flowering time regulation in Arachis. These include the Autonomous Pathway, the Aging Pathway, the Vernalization Pathway (response to cold), the Gibberellin Pathway, the Temperature Pathway, and Sugar Signaling Pathways.3 Phytohormones, in general, play multifaceted roles in plant development, including the regulation of flowering time. Specific hormonal influences are emerging in peanut; for example, brassinosteroids are implicated in the floral effects of the AhMYB51 gene.18 Furthermore, analysis of AhCOL gene promoters revealed an abundance of cis-regulatory elements responsive to abscisic acid (ABA) and methyl jasmonate (MeJA).10 This finding suggests a direct molecular mechanism by which stress hormones, which accumulate under adverse conditions like drought or pathogen attack, could modulate the expression of key photoperiod pathway genes (AhCOLs). Such a connection would allow environmental stresses to directly influence or override the primary photoperiodic signal, potentially serving as an adaptive strategy to adjust flowering time under unfavorable conditions to enhance reproductive success.

WRKY transcription factors appear to function as crucial integrators within this network. Studies indicate their involvement across a remarkable breadth of flowering pathways in Arachis, encompassing aging, autonomous, circadian clock, hormone, photoperiod, sugar, temperature, and vernalization pathways.5 Flowering is not merely a response to day length but is modulated by the plant's internal state (age, nutrient levels) and external conditions (temperature, stress). WRKY TFs, known for their roles in stress responses and development, seem ideally positioned to act as convergence points for these diverse signals. Their interaction with downstream regulators like FUL (involved in floral promotion) and AP2 (a floral repressor) 14 further supports this integrative role, potentially allowing WRKYs to fine-tune the final decision to flower based on a holistic assessment of internal and external cues. This positions WRKY TFs as key modulators capable of integrating information from multiple signaling streams to orchestrate the appropriate flowering response in peanut.

Key protein-protein interactions that form the nodes of these regulatory networks are beginning to be identified in peanut. These include the aforementioned interactions involving AhCOL1, AhCOL3, AhNF-YC1, AhCOP1, WRKY TFs, FUL, and AP2.14 Understanding these interactions is crucial for deciphering the precise molecular mechanisms controlling the floral transition.

6. Essential Databases and Resources for Peanut Flowering Time Research

Research into the genetic control of flowering time in peanut relies heavily on access to comprehensive and well-curated genomic and genetic databases. Several key resources serve the peanut research community:

  • PeanutBase (peanutbase.org): This database stands as the central, dedicated repository for genetic and genomic information pertaining to cultivated peanut (Arachis hypogaea) and its wild relatives.31 It integrates a wide array of data types, including reference genome sequences, gene predictions with functional annotations, genetic linkage maps, molecular markers (such as SNPs), QTL data, germplasm information, and extensive gene expression datasets.12 PeanutBase offers valuable bioinformatics tools, such as gene search capabilities (by keyword or identifier, e.g., arahy.XXXXXX), sequence similarity searching (BLAST), synteny viewers, and integrated genome browsers.33 Notably, it hosts RNA-Seq data from 22 different tissues of the reference cultivar 'Tifrunner', providing a rich resource for examining tissue-specific gene expression patterns.34
  • NCBI (National Center for Biotechnology Information): NCBI provides a broader biological context through its suite of databases. The NCBI Gene database offers detailed information on specific genes, including orthologs in other species, functional summaries, links to relevant literature (PubMed), and associated sequences.29 NCBI maintains reference genome assemblies and annotations for Arachis hypogaea and related species, which undergo periodic updates (e.g., Annotation Release 100).38 The Sequence Read Archive (SRA) provides access to raw sequencing data from various experiments, including transcriptomic studies relevant to flowering or stress responses.36
  • Plant Flowering Gene Database (PFGD) (pfgd.bio2db.com): This specialized database focuses specifically on flowering-time genes across a wide range of angiosperm species.40 It organizes genes based on their involvement in established flowering pathways (e.g., photoperiod, autonomous, vernalization, hormone pathways).40 As of early 2024, PFGD reportedly contained entries for 576 flowering-time associated genes from Arachis hypogaea cv. Tifrunner.5 The database structure allows users to browse by species or pathway and potentially download gene lists.40
  • Other Resources: Additional specialized resources include Ensembl Genomes, which also hosts the Arachis hypogaea genome 41, and dedicated expression atlases like the Peanut Gene Expression Atlas (AhGEA) 42 and the Arachis hypogaea Gene Expression database (AhNGE).43

Effective research into a complex trait like flowering time necessitates the synergistic use of these diverse resources. PeanutBase provides the critical species-specific genomic framework and curated data. NCBI offers broader comparative context, literature access, and raw data repositories. PFGD delivers a valuable functional overlay by organizing genes within known flowering pathways. Researchers must often navigate and integrate information from these multiple platforms to construct a comprehensive understanding. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of genomics research means these databases are continually updated with new genome assemblies, refined gene annotations, and additional experimental data.36 Consequently, researchers must remain cognizant of database versions and annotation releases to ensure they are utilizing the most current and accurate information available.

7. Implications for Peanut Improvement and Future Research Directions

The expanding knowledge of the genes and genomic regions controlling flowering time in peanut holds significant implications for crop improvement strategies and guides future research endeavors.

Breeding Applications:

The identification of candidate genes and validated QTLs associated with flowering time and related maturity traits provides tangible targets for molecular breeding. SNPs closely linked to major QTLs, such as those identified on chromosome A09 for RMSP 18 or the TTM QTLs on A04 and B03 1, can be converted into user-friendly molecular markers. These markers enable Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS), allowing breeders to screen progeny more efficiently and accurately for desirable flowering characteristics, thereby accelerating the development of improved cultivars.1 However, the polygenic nature of flowering time, evidenced by GWAS studies identifying numerous loci with small individual effects 15, suggests that relying solely on MAS for a few major genes may not be sufficient to achieve substantial shifts in flowering behavior. In such cases, Genomic Selection (GS), which utilizes genome-wide marker data to predict the breeding value of individuals, may prove more powerful for accumulating favorable alleles from multiple minor loci simultaneously. The complex genetic architecture therefore necessitates a combined approach, integrating traditional breeding with advanced molecular tools like MAS and GS, underpinned by robust phenotyping and functional gene knowledge.

Developing varieties with optimized flowering times, particularly early-maturing types, is a key objective for enhancing peanut adaptation, especially in regions with limited water availability where early maturity facilitates drought escape.1 Genes identified through GWAS or those underlying TTM QTLs 1 represent prime targets for breeding efforts aimed at achieving earliness. Furthermore, understanding how stress response pathways, potentially mediated by WRKY TFs or involving ABA/MeJA-responsive AhCOL genes, intersect with flowering control pathways is crucial. This knowledge can guide the development of cultivars that maintain stable flowering patterns even under challenging environmental conditions.

Exploiting Genetic Diversity:

The cultivated peanut germplasm suffers from a relatively narrow genetic base. Wild Arachis species, however, harbor significant genetic diversity, including potentially novel alleles influencing flowering time, as demonstrated by the major QTL identified on LG06 from an interspecific cross. Continued exploration of wild relatives and the strategic introgression of beneficial alleles into elite breeding lines remain valuable avenues for broadening the genetic toolkit available for flowering time manipulation.

Future Research Directions:

Despite considerable progress, many identified genes remain candidates requiring functional validation. Techniques such as gene editing (e.g., CRISPR/Cas9), overexpression studies, and gene silencing are essential to definitively confirm the roles of genes like AhMYB51, the GWAS candidates, and the atypically behaving TFL1-like PEBPs.8 Elucidating the complete molecular mechanisms, including characterizing protein-protein interaction networks (e.g., extending work on COL, WRKY interactions) and identifying key cis-regulatory elements controlling the expression of major flowering genes, is critical.

Further investigation into the intricate links between flowering time regulation and peanut's unique geocarpic development is warranted. Understanding the specific roles of gene families like AhFAR1 2 or light-responsive AhCOL genes in coordinating aerial flowering with subsequent subterranean podding could reveal novel regulatory checkpoints.

A major frontier lies in dissecting the molecular basis of Genotype x Environment (GxE) interactions for flowering time. Peanut is cultivated across diverse latitudes and climates, experiencing varied photoperiod and temperature regimes. Understanding which specific genes and alleles confer broad adaptation versus niche-specific flowering responses is crucial for developing varieties tailored to target environments. This requires studying allele-specific responses to environmental cues and characterizing the functional consequences of allelic variation within key flowering time genes across diverse germplasm. Such knowledge is fundamental for enabling precision breeding strategies that optimize flowering time for specific production scenarios.

8. Conclusion

The timing of flowering in peanut (Arachis hypogaea) is a complex, quantitatively inherited trait of profound agronomic importance, governed by an intricate network of genes interacting with environmental signals. Research efforts employing QTL mapping, GWAS, transcriptomics, and comparative genomics have made substantial strides in identifying key genetic players and pathways involved in this process.

Major gene families, including CONSTANS-like (COL), Phosphatidylethanolamine-Binding Proteins (PEBP, encompassing FT and TFL1 homologs), WRKY, R2R3-MYB, bHLH, FAR1, AP2/ERF, Phytochromes (PHY), and GIGANTEA (GI), have been implicated in peanut flowering control. These families contribute to various regulatory pathways, with the photoperiod pathway being particularly prominent, alongside autonomous, hormone-mediated, and stress-responsive pathways. The identification of interactions between proteins from different families (e.g., WRKY-AP2/FUL, COL-NF-YC/COP1) underscores the highly interconnected nature of the flowering regulatory network, suggesting complex signal integration rather than simple linear cascades.

Genomic studies have pinpointed specific QTLs and candidate genes associated with flowering time and related traits like time-to-maturation and pod number. While some major QTLs, potentially introgressed from wild relatives, offer targets for marker-assisted selection, the overall polygenic architecture highlights the likely need for genomic selection approaches to effectively manipulate this trait. The allotetraploid nature of peanut adds complexity, with evidence for subgenome dominance and functional divergence among homoeologous flowering genes providing both challenges and opportunities for breeding. Furthermore, peanut's unique geocarpy introduces specific selective pressures and likely involves co-option or modification of flowering-related pathways (e.g., light signaling via PHY and FAR1) for subterranean fruit development.

The availability of genomic resources like PeanutBase and NCBI, coupled with specialized databases like PFGD, provides an essential foundation for ongoing research. Future efforts should focus on the functional validation of candidate genes, particularly those with potentially novel roles in peanut, and the detailed characterization of allelic variation within these genes across diverse germplasm. Understanding the functional impact of different alleles and their interaction with environmental factors (GxE) remains a critical frontier. This knowledge is essential for developing precision breeding strategies to optimize flowering time, enhance adaptation to diverse environments, improve stress resilience, and ultimately increase the yield and stability of this vital global crop.
