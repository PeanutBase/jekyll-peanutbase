Molecular Markers for Leaf Spot Resistance Traits in Peanut Breeding

Executive Summary

Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.) is a globally significant crop, providing essential nutrition and oil, but its production is severely hampered by fungal diseases, particularly early leaf spot (ELS) and late leaf spot (LLS).1 These diseases cause substantial yield losses, often exceeding 50%, and compromise overall crop quality and economic sustainability.3 While traditional management practices, including fungicide applications, are employed, their effectiveness is limited, often leading to overuse and persistent yield constraints.1 This ongoing challenge highlights an urgent and growing demand for intrinsic, genetic solutions to enhance resistance. The limitations of chemical controls, whether due to insufficient efficacy, environmental concerns, or economic impracticality in various regions, underscore the imperative for more sustainable and inherent resistance mechanisms within the plant itself.

Recent advancements in molecular genetics and genomics have fundamentally transformed peanut breeding, marking a significant shift from traditional, often slow and resource-intensive, phenotypic selection to precise and accelerated genetic improvement.7 This evolution is critical for addressing global food security challenges, which are increasingly exacerbated by climate change and heightened disease pressure. The availability of high-resolution genetic maps, the sequencing of the peanut genome, and the development of high-throughput genotyping technologies such as Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) and Kompetitive Allele-Specific PCR (KASP) assays are enabling more efficient identification and deployment of resistance traits.10 Key molecular targets include Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs) and specific Resistance genes (R-genes) that govern defense responses.6 Furthermore, wild Arachis species serve as invaluable genetic resources, providing crucial alleles for strong resistance that are often lacking in cultivated varieties.10 Genomic databases like PeanutBase play a pivotal role in disseminating this genetic information, thereby accelerating the development of resilient peanut cultivars worldwide.16

1. Introduction to Peanut Leaf Spot Diseases

1.1. Global Significance and Economic Impact

Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.), also known as groundnut, stands as a cornerstone of global agriculture, widely cultivated in warm climates across over a hundred countries.14 It is a critical source of nutritious food, edible oil, and essential vitamins and minerals, playing a vital role in food security and economic sustainability, particularly in developing nations where it often serves as a key cash crop.2 The United States, China, India, and Nigeria are among the leading producers, with significant cultivation areas in the South and North Americas, Asia, and Africa.2

Despite its global importance, peanut production faces substantial challenges from various biotic and abiotic stresses, with fungal diseases being a primary constraint.1 Among these, leaf spot diseases, encompassing Early Leaf Spot (ELS) and Late Leaf Spot (LLS), are the most prevalent and economically devastating.1 ELS is caused by Passalora arachidicola, while LLS is caused by Nothopassalora personata.3 These diseases lead to significant yield reductions, with estimates ranging from 10% to a staggering 80% depending on environmental conditions and cultivar susceptibility.4 In severe epidemics, leaf spot diseases can cause complete defoliation and plant death, resulting in yield losses of 50% or more.3 For instance, late leaf spot alone has been reported to reduce yields by 50% to 70% in some regions.5 Beyond direct yield loss, the diseases also compromise seed size, oil content, and overall quality, impacting the marketability and economic value of the crop.2

Traditional disease management strategies often rely on intensive fungicide spray programs.6 However, these methods have inherent limitations, including the overuse and wastage of agrochemicals, as well as reduced effectiveness against certain diseases that affect root systems, where chemicals may not penetrate adequately.1 The continued persistence of significant yield losses despite these traditional approaches underscores the pressing need for more efficient and sustainable solutions.1 The wide variability in reported yield losses, from 10% to 80%, suggests that the impact of leaf spot diseases is not uniform across all regions or seasons.4 This variability is influenced by factors such as local climate (humidity, temperature), soil conditions, the specific pathogen isolates present, and the intensity of fungicide application.1 This observation highlights that a single, universal solution for resistance breeding might not be sufficient; instead, breeding programs must develop regionally adapted disease management strategies and resistant varieties that perform robustly across diverse environmental contexts. The substantial economic losses incurred from these diseases, coupled with the environmental and financial burdens of chemical control, create a powerful economic incentive for investing in genetically resistant varieties.1 This strategic pivot from chemical dependence to genetic solutions represents a more sustainable, environmentally benign, and economically viable long-term approach for the global peanut industry.2

1.2. Early Leaf Spot (ELS) and Late Leaf Spot (LLS): Pathogens, Symptoms, and Disease Cycle

Understanding the specific characteristics of ELS and LLS is crucial for effective disease management and resistance breeding. Both are fungal diseases that significantly impact peanut foliage.

Pathogens:

Early leaf spot is caused by Passalora arachidicola, previously known as Cercospora arachidicola.10 Late leaf spot is caused by Nothopassalora personata, formerly Cercosporidium personatum, and also known as Phaeoisariopsis personata.3 The sexual stage (teleomorph) for N. personata is Mycosphaerella berkeleyi, though its observation in nature is rare.3

Symptoms:

The symptoms of ELS and LLS, while distinct, can sometimes be challenging to differentiate, especially in early stages or in fields with mixed infections.

  • Early Leaf Spot (ELS): ELS typically presents as penetrating brown lesions or spots visible on both the upper and lower leaf surfaces.14 These spots are commonly surrounded by a light yellow halo, although the presence of a halo is not exclusively diagnostic as it can also occur with LLS.18 During periods of high humidity, the fungus produces characteristic tufts of silvery, hair-like spores, which can be observed on the upper surface of the lesions, often with the aid of a magnifying glass.18 ELS can appear as early as 30 days after planting, though it is usually observed later in well-rotated fields.18
  • Late Leaf Spot (LLS): LLS lesions are typically dark brown to black and penetrate through the leaf, making them visible on both upper and lower surfaces.3 A distinguishing feature of LLS is the dense mass of dark spores on the underside (abaxial surface) of the leaf, which gives the spot a velvety appearance and can often be seen without magnification.3 LLS symptoms generally appear later in the growing season, typically between 30 to 50 days after planting.4 Lesions are circular, ranging from 1 to 10 mm in diameter, and can coalesce.3

Differentiating between ELS and LLS, particularly early in the season, can be difficult due to their subtle symptomatic differences and the common occurrence of mixed infections.3 Visual diagnosis can be confounded by spots caused by pesticide injuries or other irregular leaf spots.18 Confirmation of diagnosis relies on the presence and specific location of spores (upper surface for ELS, lower for LLS), which can be encouraged by placing symptomatic leaves in a moist chamber for a day or two to induce sporulation.18 The subtle symptomatic differences between ELS and LLS and the common occurrence of mixed infections necessitate careful and often labor-intensive diagnosis.3 This diagnostic challenge highlights the significant value of molecular markers, which offer a more precise, rapid, and objective identification method for breeding programs, bypassing the need for subjective visual assessment or specific conditions for sporulation.18

Disease Cycle:

Both ELS and LLS fungi overwinter in peanut crop debris from previous seasons.3 Old lesions on infected leaves, stems, and petioles serve as the primary source of initial inoculum.3 Spores (conidia) produced from these lesions are then dispersed by air currents, wind, or splashing water onto newly planted peanut leaves, initiating new infection cycles.3 The presence of abundant peanut residue and infected volunteer plants in fields with short rotations significantly contributes to the early and rapid development of leaf spot diseases.3

Environmental Conditions:

High humidity and warm temperatures are highly conducive to the development and spread of both leaf spot diseases.

  • For ELS, temperatures between approximately 65 and 90 degrees Fahrenheit (18-32°C) are very favorable for infection.18
  • For LLS, optimal conditions include temperatures near 20°C and prolonged periods (12 hours or more) of high relative humidity (>93%) or leaf wetness.3 Little disease develops if temperatures are outside the 15-28°C range or if leaf wetness periods are insufficient.3 Peanut rust, another significant fungal disease, also thrives in warm and humid conditions (20–30 °C).2
  • Under favorable conditions, lesions typically become visible around 10 to 12 days after infection for ELS, with sporulation beginning 2 to 4 days later and continuing for up to two weeks.18 For LLS, mature sporulating lesions may appear 14 to 24 days after infection.3 Multiple secondary infection cycles can occur within a single growing season, leading to a rapid increase in disease severity.3 The consistent environmental conditions favoring leaf spot development, particularly high humidity and warm temperatures, highlight the importance of breeding for broad-spectrum resistance or resistance that is robust across diverse agro-climatic zones.1 This also suggests that climate change, potentially leading to more favorable conditions in new or expanded regions, could exacerbate the problem, making genetic resistance even more critical for future peanut production and global food security.

2. Genetic Architecture of Leaf Spot Resistance

2.1. Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs) Governing ELS and LLS Resistance

Resistance to leaf spot diseases in peanut is a complex genetic trait, characterized by its polygenic nature, meaning it is controlled by multiple major and minor Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs).6 Significant advancements have been made in identifying and precisely mapping these QTLs for both ELS and LLS resistance through the use of various genetic mapping populations, such as Recombinant Inbred Lines (RILs), and the application of advanced molecular marker systems.6

Key QTLs identified for LLS resistance include:

  • qLLSB05: This QTL, located on chromosome B05, has demonstrated remarkable consistency across multi-year analyses, explaining a substantial proportion of the phenotypic variation (PVE) ranging from 15% to 41%.6 This high PVE indicates a strong genetic effect, meaning that selecting for this QTL will have a significant impact on resistance. Its consistent expression over multiple years further enhances its reliability and utility in breeding programs, suggesting stable genetic control rather than environmentally sensitive expression, which directly translates to more efficient and predictable resistance breeding. Notably, qLLSB05 is anchored by two NBS-LRR resistance genes, indicating a direct genetic link to specific defense mechanisms.10
  • qLLSA05: Found on chromosome A05, this QTL contributes between 7% and 10% of the phenotypic variation.6
  • qLLSB03: Located on chromosome B03, this QTL explains 5% to 7% of the phenotypic variation.6 The presence of resistance alleles in both qLLSB03 and qLLSB05 loci has been correlated with protection from yield loss caused by LLS disease damage.6
  • Other major QTLs for LLS resistance have been reported on linkage groups AhXV (now designated A03) and AhXII (now A02).7
  • A major QTL for LLS resistance, qLLSA02 on chromosome A02, has been specifically associated with resistance in the CS16 variety, a line known to derive its resistance from the wild species Arachis cardenasii.20

Key QTLs identified for ELS resistance include:

  • Three resistance QTLs have been consistently identified across multiple years: two on chromosome A03 (PVE = 8–12%) and one on chromosome B03 (PVE = 13–20%).6
  • The locus qELSA03_1.1 on chromosome A03 is particularly noteworthy as it coincides with a previously published genomic region associated with LLS resistance in the GPBD4 variety.6 This overlap suggests potential shared genetic pathways or closely linked resistance genes that could confer resistance to both diseases.
  • Recent studies using an interspecific AB-QTL population have identified five QTLs for ELS resistance on chromosomes A02, A03, A08, B04, and B09.15 A significant finding from these studies is that favorable alleles at all but the B09 QTL were contributed by wild species, highlighting the critical role of genetic diversity from wild relatives. This observation reinforces the necessity of ongoing efforts to introgress these alleles into cultivated peanut, especially given the narrow genetic base of Arachis hypogaea. This strategy is essential for ensuring long-term resistance durability and preventing resistance breakdown, as relying solely on existing cultivated germplasm may not provide sufficient genetic variation for sustainable resistance.
  • A specific genomic region on chromosome A02 has been identified as containing several relevant QTLs, contributing not only to ELS resistance but also to desirable agronomic traits such as earliness and increased biomass yield.15 The co-localization of QTLs for ELS resistance with other desirable agronomic traits presents a significant opportunity for multi-trait selection in breeding. This "trait stacking" or "pyramiding" using a single genomic region can dramatically improve breeding efficiency by simultaneously selecting for disease resistance and yield-enhancing characteristics, leading to the development of more commercially viable cultivars.21
  • Two major QTLs for ELS resistance have been reported on chromosomes A03 and B04, and these are associated with NBS-LRR resistance genes.10
  • Other studies have assigned major QTLs for ELS resistance to linkage groups A01 and A03.7

Comparative analysis of the genomic regions spanning these QTLs suggests that resistance to ELS and LLS is largely genetically independent.6 This finding has significant implications for breeding strategies. It suggests that breeders cannot assume that selecting for resistance to one leaf spot disease will automatically confer resistance to the other. Therefore, pyramiding resistance genes for both diseases might require targeting distinct genomic regions, potentially increasing the complexity but also the specificity of breeding efforts. However, the occasional overlap, such as qELSA03_1.1 coinciding with LLS resistance, offers opportunities for selecting for both traits simultaneously, potentially streamlining breeding efforts.6

2.2. Identification and Role of Resistance Genes (R-genes)

Resistance genes (R-genes) are a fundamental component of the plant immune system, playing a critical role in recognizing pathogen-derived molecules and initiating defense responses. These genes have been successfully identified in peanut, providing specific molecular targets for enhancing disease resistance.14

A comprehensive study involving advanced breeding lines evaluated 76 candidate R-genes in peanut. This research revealed that 36 of these R-genes were differentially expressed and significantly correlated with resistant lines.14 The majority of these identified R-genes belong to the Receptor-Like Kinases (RLKs) and Receptor-Like Proteins (RLPs) classes. These proteins are crucial for sensing the presence of pathogens at the cell surface, acting as the plant's first line of defense by recognizing pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and initiating a protective response.14

The largest group among the identified R-genes was Receptor-Like Cytoplasmic Kinases (RLCKs) VII. These RLCKs are known to be involved in pattern-triggered kinase signaling, a key pathway in plant immunity that leads to the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS).14 ROS generation is a rapid and potent defense mechanism, contributing to localized cell death and strengthening of cell walls to restrict pathogen spread. The identification of specific R-gene classes and their known mechanistic roles in pathogen sensing and defense initiation provides a deeper, mechanistic understanding of peanut resistance. This moves beyond simply identifying a genomic region (QTL) to understanding the underlying biological pathways, which can inform more targeted gene editing or functional genomics approaches in breeding for enhanced and durable resistance.

Intriguingly, four of the identified R-genes showed homology to the TMV resistant protein N, which has been demonstrated to confer resistance against tobacco mosaic virus.14 This observation suggests the presence of conserved defense mechanisms across different plant species and against diverse pathogens, potentially indicating broad-spectrum resistance capabilities. When these 36 R-genes were mapped to the peanut genomes, they were found to be widely distributed across most chromosomes, with the exceptions of A09 and B09.14

A notable observation was the low levels of gene expression in resistant lines for these R-genes.14 This suggests a finely tuned regulatory mechanism where the plant balances defense activation with overall growth and productivity. This implies that simply overexpressing R-genes might not be the optimal strategy for breeding; rather, breeders might need to select for alleles that confer effective resistance without imposing a significant fitness cost, ensuring that resistant varieties maintain good agronomic traits and yield potential.14 This represents a crucial trade-off in plant breeding, as high resistance is desirable, but if it comes at the expense of yield or other agronomic traits, it may not be practical for farmers. Therefore, the most effective resistance alleles are those that provide adequate defense while minimizing metabolic burden, making it a multi-objective optimization problem in breeding. Furthermore, some major QTLs for resistance, such as the LLS resistance QTL on chromosome B05, are specifically anchored by NBS-LRR resistance genes 10, further solidifying the direct link between specific R-genes and phenotypic resistance to leaf spot diseases.

3. Molecular Markers for Leaf Spot Resistance Traits

3.1. Overview of Marker Systems in Peanut (SNPs, SSRs, InDels, RFLPs, etc.)

Molecular markers have become indispensable tools in modern plant breeding, significantly expediting the selection process for desired traits. In peanut, these markers have been successfully employed for improving characteristics such as high oleic acid content and rust resistance.7 Over the years, a diverse array of marker systems has been utilized in peanut genetic studies and breeding programs. These include Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphisms (RFLPs), Random Amplified Polymorphic DNAs (RAPDs), Amplified Fragment Length Polymorphisms (AFLPs), Simple Sequence Repeats (SSRs), Inter Simple Sequence Repeats (ISSRs), Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs), and Insertion/Deletion (InDel) markers.7

Historically, RFLPs were among the first marker systems to reveal a large number of polymorphisms and were widely used for creating linkage maps and implementing indirect selection strategies.24 However, cultivated peanut generally exhibited limited molecular variation when analyzed with RFLP technology.24 Similarly, while nearly 10,000 SSR markers have been identified globally, their widespread application in peanut breeding has been constrained by low levels of polymorphism, with only about 14.5% showing polymorphism and 6.4% being mapped.23 This low polymorphism is primarily attributed to the extremely narrow genetic basis of cultivated peanut.23 This persistent low level of polymorphism in cultivated peanut represents a significant bottleneck for marker-assisted breeding. This inherent genetic limitation necessitates the strategic development and adoption of more polymorphic marker systems like SNPs and InDels, and critically, the utilization of wild relatives to introduce greater genetic diversity into breeding populations.9 This highlights the importance of leveraging external genetic resources to overcome intrinsic genetic constraints.

In recent years, Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) have emerged as a more popular and user-friendly marker type.20 SNPs offer significantly higher density and mapping resolution, particularly with the advent of next-generation sequencing (NGS)-based genotyping.9 High-density SNP panels, such as those containing 2500 SNP markers distributed across the 20 groundnut chromosomes, are now available, providing powerful tools for breeders to estimate background genome recovery, conduct diversity analyses, and manage germplasm efficiently.12

Insertion/Deletion (InDel) markers are also increasingly recognized as an abundant source of genetic markers widely distributed across the genome.9 They have been reported to exhibit higher polymorphism than SSRs in some crops and have been successfully developed from functional genes in peanut.9 Five InDel markers derived from functional genes have been identified as significantly correlated to leaf spot resistance, demonstrating their potential utility in identifying disease-resistant genotypes within breeding populations.23

Kompetitive Allele-Specific PCR (KASP) assays, a fluorescence-based SNP genotyping system, are being developed and validated for their cost-effectiveness and suitability for large-scale genotyping in modern breeding programs.11 This clear technological shift towards high-density SNP maps and KASP assays signifies a move towards more efficient, precise, and cost-effective large-scale genotyping. This advancement is not merely about finding more markers, but about making marker-assisted selection (MAS) practical and scalable for routine breeding programs, thereby fundamentally accelerating the rate of cultivar development.8 This evolution in marker technology and genotyping methods directly addresses the practical limitations of traditional breeding. High-density maps provide better resolution for QTL mapping, while cost-effective, high-throughput genotyping makes MAS feasible for large breeding populations. This means that molecular breeding is moving from a research-intensive activity to a more routine, industrialized process, democratizing its application in breeding programs worldwide.

3.2. Specific QTLs and Linked Markers for Early Leaf Spot Resistance

Significant progress has been made in identifying specific QTLs and developing linked molecular markers for Early Leaf Spot (ELS) resistance, which are crucial for accelerating peanut breeding.

QTLs for ELS Resistance:

  • Recent studies using interspecific populations have mapped five QTLs for ELS resistance on chromosomes A02, A03, A08, B04, and B09.15 A key finding is that favorable alleles for these QTLs were primarily contributed by wild Arachis species, underscoring their importance as genetic resources.15
  • A particularly significant genomic region on chromosome A02 has been identified, containing multiple QTLs that contribute not only to ELS resistance but also to desirable agronomic traits such as earliness and increased biomass yield.15 This co-localization of QTLs for ELS resistance with other desirable agronomic traits presents a significant opportunity for multi-trait selection in breeding. This "trait stacking" or "pyramiding" using a single genomic region can dramatically improve breeding efficiency by simultaneously selecting for disease resistance and yield-enhancing characteristics, leading to the development of more commercially viable cultivars.21
  • Two QTLs on chromosome A03 have been identified, explaining 8–12% of the phenotypic variation for ELS resistance, along with one on chromosome B03, explaining 13–20% of the phenotypic variation.6
  • One major QTL on A03, designated qELSA03_1.1, is notable for its co-occurrence with a genomic region previously associated with LLS resistance, suggesting potential shared genetic pathways or closely linked resistance genes.6
  • Two major QTLs for ELS resistance have been reported on chromosomes A03 and B04, and these are associated with NBS-LRR resistance genes, indicating a direct genetic basis for the resistance.10
  • Other research has assigned major QTLs for ELS resistance to linkage groups A01 and A03.7

Molecular Markers for ELS Resistance:

  • While specific SNP marker names for ELS resistance are not explicitly listed as validated markers on the provided PeanutBase's Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) page 15, the Axiom-Arachis 48K SNPs chip has been extensively utilized for genotyping in studies that successfully mapped ELS QTLs.15 This high-density SNP array provides a robust platform for identifying markers linked to resistance.
  • New SNP markers are continuously being developed and placed on genetic maps, with ongoing efforts to fill existing gaps and identify additional QTLs for both early and late leaf spot resistance.27 For instance, a revised genetic linkage map for an interspecific introgression population of peanut has been developed from SNP markers, with studies underway to identify additional QTLs for ELS and LLS resistance.27
  • Five InDel markers derived from functional genes have been identified as significantly correlated to leaf spot, demonstrating their potential utility in identifying disease-resistant genotypes within breeding populations.23 These InDels, identified from conserved sequences of functional genes, offer a promising avenue for marker-assisted selection, particularly given their reported higher polymorphism compared to SSRs in some crops.23

3.3. Specific QTLs and Linked Markers for Late Leaf Spot Resistance

Extensive research has focused on identifying and validating QTLs and their associated molecular markers for Late Leaf Spot (LLS) resistance, a critical step in developing resistant peanut varieties.

QTLs for LLS Resistance:

  • Major QTLs for LLS resistance have been consistently identified on chromosome B05, notably qLLSB05, which explains a significant proportion of phenotypic variation (15–41%) and is anchored by two NBS-LRR resistance genes.6 The high phenotypic variation explained by qLLSB05 and its consistent expression over multi-year analyses makes it a highly reliable and valuable target for marker-assisted selection in breeding programs.6 This indicates a strong and stable genetic control over LLS resistance, allowing for more predictable breeding outcomes.
  • Other significant QTLs include qLLSA05 on chromosome A05 (PVE = 7–10%) and qLLSB03 on chromosome B03 (PVE = 5–7%).6 The presence of resistance alleles at qLLSB03 and qLLSB05 loci has been directly correlated with protection from yield loss caused by LLS disease.6
  • Additional major QTLs for LLS resistance have been reported on linkage groups AhXV (now designated A03) and AhXII (now A02).7
  • The cultivated peanut variety CS16 (ICGV 86855) is a well-known source of LLS resistance, with its resistance primarily traced to the wild diploid peanut Arachis cardenasii.20 A major QTL, qLLSA02 on chromosome A02, has been identified as conferring LLS resistance in CS16.20 A. cardenasii remains the most widely used wild source of resistance to leaf spot diseases.15 This highlights the critical role of wild relatives in providing strong resistance alleles that are often limited in cultivated peanut germplasm.10 While A. cardenasii has been highly effective, the continuous use of new diploid species as sources of resistance is important to diversify options for breeders and help avoid potential resistance breakdown in the future.15

Molecular Markers for LLS Resistance:

  • Several SNP markers have been developed and validated for LLS resistance. A recent study validated three SNP markers—GKAMA02GL582, GKAMA02GL829, and GKAMA02GL975—showing strong association with LLS resistance, with adjusted R² values of 0.758, 0.73, and 0.613, respectively.20 These markers were designed from the major QTL qLLSA02 on chromosome A02, which is known to confer LLS resistance in the CS16 variety.20 The successful validation of these markers means they can be directly applied in future breeding programs to identify LLS-resistant groundnut lines, significantly speeding up the introgression of this trait.20
  • Simple Sequence Repeat (SSR) markers have also been associated with LLS resistance. For instance, in a cross between a susceptible genotype (TMV 2) and a resistant genotype (COG 0437), three SSR primers—PM 375162, pPGPseq5D5220, and PM 384100—were able to distinguish resistant and susceptible bulks and individuals for LLS.28 In single marker analysis, markers such as PM 375, PM 384, pPGPseq5D5, PM 137, PM 3, PMc 588, and Ah 4-26 were linked with LLS severity scores, explaining phenotypic variation ranging from 32% to 59%.28 The primer PM 384100 allele, in particular, showed a clear association with resistance during validation across a set of genotypes.28
  • The Groundnut DArTag SNP panel, a cost-effective mid-density genotyping panel containing 2500 SNP markers, includes 72 associated markers for 8 key traits, among which late leaf spot resistance is explicitly listed.12 This panel provides a powerful tool for groundnut breeders worldwide.
  • PeanutBase, a primary genetics and genomics database for cultivated peanut and its wild relatives, lists "Late leaf spot (Phaeoisariopsis personata infection)" as a trait for which Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) has been used, providing a valuable resource for breeders seeking information on associated markers and germplasm.26

Conclusion

The persistent and significant yield losses caused by early and late leaf spot diseases underscore the critical need for robust genetic resistance in peanut cultivation. The limitations of traditional disease management, including fungicide applications, further emphasize the urgency of developing inherently resistant varieties. Recent advancements in molecular genetics have ushered in a transformative era for peanut breeding, enabling a more precise and accelerated approach to cultivar development.

The identification and mapping of numerous Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs) for both ELS and LLS resistance represent a cornerstone of this progress. Major QTLs, such as qLLSB05 on chromosome B05 for LLS and regions on chromosomes A02 and A03 for ELS, explain substantial phenotypic variation and demonstrate consistent expression, making them highly reliable targets for marker-assisted selection. The detailed understanding of specific Resistance genes (R-genes), including various Receptor-Like Kinases (RLKs) and Receptor-Like Proteins (RLPs), provides a deeper mechanistic insight into peanut's defense responses, paving the way for more targeted breeding strategies.

The evolution of molecular marker systems, particularly the shift towards high-density Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) maps and cost-effective Kompetitive Allele-Specific PCR (KASP) assays, has overcome historical limitations posed by the narrow genetic base of cultivated peanut. These advanced tools facilitate large-scale, high-throughput genotyping, making marker-assisted selection a practical and scalable component of routine breeding programs. The successful validation of specific SNP markers, such as GKAMA02GL582, GKAMA02GL829, and GKAMA02GL975 for LLS resistance, directly supports their immediate application in identifying resistant lines. Similarly, the identification of promising InDel markers further diversifies the molecular toolkit available to breeders.

Crucially, the recognition that wild Arachis species contribute favorable resistance alleles for many QTLs highlights their indispensable role as a genetic reservoir. Ongoing efforts to introgress this rich genetic diversity into cultivated peanut are vital for ensuring long-term resistance durability and preventing resistance breakdown. Furthermore, the discovery of genomic regions that confer resistance to multiple diseases or co-localize with other desirable agronomic traits, such as earliness and increased biomass yield, presents significant opportunities for multi-trait selection and trait pyramiding, leading to the development of more commercially viable and resilient cultivars.

In conclusion, the integration of molecular markers and genomic insights into peanut breeding programs is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamental paradigm shift. This approach offers a sustainable, environmentally sound, and economically viable strategy to combat leaf spot diseases, ensuring enhanced productivity and quality for the global peanut industry in the face of evolving environmental and pathogenic challenges. Continued investment in genomic research, marker development, and the utilization of wild genetic resources will be paramount for future advancements in peanut breeding.
