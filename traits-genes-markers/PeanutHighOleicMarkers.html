Molecular Markers and Their Sequences Associated with the High Oleic Acid Trait in Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.)

1. Introduction to High Oleic Acid in Peanut

Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.) stands as a globally significant oilseed crop, valued for its substantial protein and oil content.1 Within its seeds, the fatty acid composition is a critical determinant of quality, with oleic acid (OA) being a particularly important monounsaturated omega-9 fatty acid.3 The presence of high oleic acid content in peanut oil confers numerous advantages, both for industrial applications and human health.

From an industrial perspective, high oleic acid significantly enhances the oxidative stability of peanut oil, thereby extending its shelf life and improving flavor.1 This superior stability is attributed to oleic acid's resistance to auto-oxidation, which is reported to be 10-fold higher than that of polyunsaturated linoleic acid (LA).2 The oleic-to-linoleic acid (O/L) ratio serves as a key indicator of this quality. While normal peanut genotypes typically exhibit an O/L ratio ranging from 1.0 to 4.0, high O/L mutant lines can achieve ratios as high as 30 to 40.5 An O/L ratio exceeding 9, or an oleic acid content above 70%, is generally considered industrially desirable.8

Beyond its industrial utility, high oleic acid offers notable health benefits. Consumption of oleic acid has been linked to improved human health outcomes, including the reduction of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol while maintaining high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels, anti-inflammatory effects, and a reduced risk of coronary heart disease, inflammatory diseases, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and hypertension.2 These health advantages contribute to the growing consumer demand for healthier edible oils. Consequently, increasing oleic acid levels in peanut has become a central objective in breeding programs, driven by both market demands for high-quality vegetable oil and public health considerations.1 The economic implications are substantial, with a mere 1% increase in peanut oil content potentially leading to a 7% surge in oil processing revenue.4 This underscores that the scientific pursuit of molecular markers and gene editing is not purely academic but is fundamentally driven by significant economic and public health imperatives, highlighting the translational value of this research.

Peanut seeds typically contain approximately 50% oil, with oleic and linoleic acids constituting about 80% of the total fatty acids.5 The biosynthesis of fatty acids involves a crucial step where oleic acid (C18:1) is converted to linoleic acid (C18:2). This conversion is catalyzed by Δ12 fatty acid desaturase (FAD2) enzymes, also known as oleate dehydrogenase, which introduce a second double bond at the C12 position of oleic acid.5 Mutations within the FAD2 gene lead to a decrease in the activity of this enzyme, thereby impeding the synthesis of linoleic acid and resulting in an accumulation of oleic acid in peanut seeds.6

The high oleic acid trait in cultivated peanut is predominantly controlled by recessive alleles of two homeologous genes: ahFAD2A (also referred to as ol1) and ahFAD2B (also referred to as ol2).5 These two genes share a high degree of sequence homology, approximately 99% in their coding regions.8 Achieving the high oleic acid phenotype typically requires loss-of-function mutations or a significant reduction in the transcript levels of both ahFAD2A and ahFAD2B.5 This necessity for dual gene inactivation explains why high oleic acid peanuts are often characterized as "double mutants".5

2. Genetic Basis: The AhFAD2 Genes

2.1. Origin and Structure of AhFAD2 Genes

Cultivated peanut, Arachis hypogaea L., is an allotetraploid species with a genome designated as AABB (2n = 4x = 40).9 This genetic constitution is a result of a natural hybridization event between two wild diploid progenitor species: Arachis duranensis, which contributed the A genome, and Arachis ipaensis, which contributed the B genome.9 The evolutionary history of peanut as an allotetraploid explains the presence of duplicated genes, known as homeologs, derived from these ancestral genomes.

Among these homeologous genes are AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B, which are derived from the A and B subgenomes, respectively.12 These two genes exhibit remarkable sequence similarity, sharing approximately 99% homology in their coding regions, with only about 11 base pair differences.8 Their coding sequences (CDS) span 1140 base pairs, encoding 379 amino acids, and notably, they lack introns in their coding regions.8 This high degree of similarity underscores their shared evolutionary origin and highly conserved functional roles in fatty acid metabolism.

2.2. Functional Role in Oleic Acid to Linoleic Acid Conversion

The primary function of AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B is to encode microsomal oleoyl-PC desaturase enzymes.9 These enzymes are integral membrane proteins located within the endoplasmic reticulum of plant cells.9 Their critical role in fatty acid biosynthesis involves catalyzing the desaturation of oleic acid (C18:1) to linoleic acid (C18:2) by introducing a second double bond.3

In wild-type or "normal" peanut genotypes, where both AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B genes are fully functional, the efficient conversion of oleic acid to linoleic acid is maintained, resulting in a typical O/L ratio ranging from 1.0 to 4.0.5 However, when mutations occur in these genes, they lead to altered gene expression and/or a reduction in the enzymatic activity of the oleoyl-PC desaturase.3 This diminished enzymatic function impedes the conversion of oleic acid, causing it to accumulate and consequently increasing the O/L ratio, thereby conferring the high oleic acid phenotype.3

The necessity for inactivating both AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B genes to produce high O/L genotypes 5 highlights a critical aspect of peanut genetics: functional redundancy. Given their high sequence homology, if only one gene were mutated, the other functional copy would likely compensate for the loss of enzyme activity, preventing the significant accumulation of oleic acid. This inherent genetic redundancy explains why the high oleic trait is a "double mutant" phenomenon, requiring breeders to target both homeologous genes simultaneously for complete inactivation and the desired high oleic acid accumulation.

2.3. Chromosomal Locations

The precise chromosomal localization of the AhFAD2 genes is fundamental for molecular breeding efforts, enabling targeted genetic manipulation.

  • AhFAD2A has been consistently mapped to the A sub-genome, specifically on linkage group a09 (chromosome A09).20
  • AhFAD2B is located on the B sub-genome, specifically on linkage group b09 (chromosome B09).20

Both genes are therefore situated on chromosome 9 of their respective subgenomes.24 For instance, a novel and stable quantitative trait locus (QTL) for oil content, designated qOCB09.1, was mapped to chromosome B09. This QTL region spans approximately 1.99 Mb and includes the FAD2B gene, indicating its influence on oil content and fatty acid composition.26 This precise mapping provides invaluable genetic targets for breeders and molecular biologists, streamlining the process of marker development and gene editing efforts.

3. Specific Molecular Mutations Conferring High Oleic Acid

The high oleic acid trait in peanut is a direct consequence of specific loss-of-function mutations in the AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B genes. These mutations either reduce or completely eliminate the activity of the oleoyl-PC desaturase enzyme, thereby preventing the conversion of oleic acid to linoleic acid.

3.1. Mutations in AhFAD2A

The most widely reported and characterized mutation in AhFAD2A (ol1) is a single nucleotide substitution of Guanine (G) to Adenine (A) at the 448 base pair (bp) position after the start codon (G448A).7 This specific G448A transition leads to a missense amino acid change, where Aspartic acid (D) is replaced by Asparagine (N) at position 150 (D150N).8 This amino acid alteration is critical as it impacts the enzyme's structural integrity and catalytic efficiency, leading to reduced desaturase activity.33 This mutation is commonly found in many natural high O/L lines.29

3.2. Mutations in AhFAD2B

The AhFAD2B gene (ol2) exhibits a greater diversity of mutations that confer the high oleic acid phenotype, indicating multiple origins for this desirable trait.

  • Natural Mutation (F435 type): The most prevalent natural mutation identified in AhFAD2B, particularly in the widely utilized high O/L line F435, is a single nucleotide 'A' insertion between base pairs 441 and 442 (441_442insA).3 This insertion causes a frameshift mutation in the coding sequence.3 The frameshift subsequently leads to the generation of a premature stop codon, resulting in the production of a truncated, non-functional protein.3 This type of mutation is highly effective in causing a complete loss of enzyme function, which is why the F435 line and its derivatives (e.g., 'Georgia-02C', 'Georgia Hi-O/L') are extensively used as donor parents in breeding programs.7
  • Other Reported Mutations: Beyond the F435 type, several other mutations in AhFAD2B have been identified, highlighting the diverse genetic origins of the high oleic trait:
  • A transposable element insertion has been characterized in the AhFAD2B gene of the cultivar 'Flavorunner 458'.7
  • A novel Guanine (G) to Thymine (T) substitution at position 451 (G451T) has been identified in AhFAD2B through CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing.16 This mutation was observed independently and not in conjunction with the 441_442insA mutation, suggesting it represents a distinct mechanism for loss of function.16
  • A Cytosine (C) to Guanine (G) substitution at position 301 (C301G) in AhFAD2B was found in natural mutant lines such as PI342666. This mutation results in a histidine (H) to aspartic acid (D) amino acid change at position 101 (H101D).8
  • Chemically induced mutations of AhFAD2B in lines M2-225 and C458 involved miniature inverted transposable element (MITE) insertions at 997 bp and 665 bp, respectively, leading to the loss of AhFAD2B function.24

The identification of multiple distinct AhFAD2B mutations (e.g., 441_442insA, transposable element insertion, G451T, C301G, MITE insertions) that all lead to the high oleic phenotype demonstrates that the trait can arise from various genetic origins. This genetic diversity is crucial for broadening the genetic base of high oleic cultivars. Given the often narrow genetic background of many existing high-oleic donor parents 24, breeders can leverage these different mutations, or induce new ones via gene editing technologies like CRISPR/Cas9 16, to introduce the high oleic trait into a wider range of elite cultivars without necessarily inheriting undesirable linked traits from a limited donor pool. The observation that natural mutations appear more frequently in the AhFAD2A gene than in the AhFAD2B gene 16 also provides valuable information that can guide targeted gene editing strategies.

4. Molecular Markers for High Oleic Acid Trait Detection

Molecular markers are indispensable tools in modern peanut breeding, enabling the rapid and efficient selection for the high oleic acid trait by circumventing the labor-intensive and time-consuming process of traditional fatty acid composition analyses (phenotyping).3 These markers accelerate breeding cycles by allowing early identification and elimination of undesirable lines, and facilitating the introgression of desired traits.3

4.1. Cleaved Amplified Polymorphic Sequence (CAPS) Markers

CAPS markers are PCR-based molecular markers that detect genetic polymorphisms by exploiting the presence or absence of a restriction endonuclease recognition site.30 The principle involves PCR amplification of a specific genomic region, followed by digestion of the PCR product with a chosen restriction enzyme. Differences in fragment sizes after digestion, visualized via gel electrophoresis, indicate the presence of specific alleles.30 CAPS markers are a robust and preferred choice for routine screening of a large number of samples in breeding programs.36 They have been successfully developed and applied for detecting allele polymorphisms in both AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B.8

For the 441_442insA mutation in AhFAD2B, a specific CAPS marker has been designed. This marker targets the insertion site 7 and utilizes the restriction enzyme Hpy188I.15 The Hpy188I enzyme recognizes the sequence 5'-TCNGA-3'.30 The 441_442insA insertion in the mutant AhFAD2B allele specifically creates a "TCAGA" site, which is then recognized and cleaved by Hpy188I, allowing for differentiation from the wild-type allele.30

The primer sequences for amplifying the region containing this mutation are:

  • Forward primer (1344): 5′-ggagctttaacaacacaa-3′ 15
  • Reverse primer (1345): 5′-atatgggagcataagggt-3′ 15

When these primers are used, the PCR product amplified from the AhFAD2B mutant allele (containing 441_442insA) is digested by Hpy188I into two fragments of 521 bp and 137 bp. The wild-type allele's amplicon remains undigested.15

An alternative primer pair, bF19/R1(FAD), is also available for the AhFAD2B 441_442insA mutation:

  • Forward primer (bF19): 5′-cagaaccattagctttg-3′ 15
  • Reverse primer (R1/FAD): 5′-ctctgactatgcatcag-3′ 15 Using these primers, digestion of the mutant allele amplicon with Hpy188I yields fragments of 505, 263, 213, 171, 32, and 12 bp. The wild-type allele amplicon yields fragments of 736, 263, 171, 32, and 12 bp.15 These bF19/R1(FAD) primers are AhFAD2B specific and can be used to detect heterozygosity, which is valuable in breeding programs.15

For the G448A mutation in AhFAD2A, the restriction enzyme Hpy99I is used.15 The wild-type AhFAD2A gene contains a Hpy99I recognition site (5'-CGWCG-3'), which, upon digestion, yields 598 bp and 228 bp fragments. In contrast, the Ahfad2a mutant allele (G448A) lacks this recognition site, and its PCR product remains undigested.30

The precise design of CAPS markers leverages the direct causal link between specific nucleotide changes (such as the 'A' insertion or G448A substitution) and the presence or absence of a restriction enzyme recognition site. This molecular interaction enables the marker to differentiate between wild-type and mutant alleles. The continued utility and development of CAPS markers, even with the advent of newer technologies, highlight their reliability and cost-effectiveness for certain breeding applications, particularly for laboratories with limited access to advanced equipment like qPCR machines.34 This demonstrates the importance of a diverse toolkit of markers to suit varying resource levels and throughput requirements.

4.2. Kompetitive Allele Specific PCR (KASP) Markers

KASP technology represents a significant advancement in molecular breeding, offering high accuracy, time-saving capabilities, high-throughput genotyping, and a relatively low cost per data point.22 A key advantage of KASP is its ability to detect both alleles in a single reaction, making it exceptionally well-suited for large-scale screening in peanut breeding programs.22 The reliability of the KASP method has been rigorously validated through comparisons with CAPS analysis, demonstrating consistent results.22

KASP assays operate on the principle of competitive binding. Two allele-specific forward primers are designed, each carrying a unique tail sequence that corresponds to a universal FRET (fluorescence resonant energy transfer) cassette. One primer is typically labeled with a FAM™ dye (designed for the mutant allele), and the other with a HEX™ dye (designed for the wild-type allele). The differential binding and subsequent fluorescent signaling allow for clear discrimination of homozygous wild-type, homozygous mutant, and heterozygous genotypes.30

The specific KASP primer sequences for AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B are as follows 30:

  • For AhFAD2A (targeting G448A mutation):
  • AhFAD2A-Allele-l (mutant allele, FAM™ dye): 5′-GTTTTGGGACAAACACTTCGTT-3′
  • AhFAD2A-Allele-2 (wild-type allele, HEX™ dye): 5′-GTTTTGGGACAAACACTTCGTC-3′
  • AhFAD2A-common (reverse primer): 5′-CGCCACCACTCCAACACC-3′
  • For AhFAD2B (targeting 441_442insA mutation):
  • AhFAD2B-Allele-l (mutant allele, FAM™ dye): 5′-CAAACACTTCGTCGCGGTCT-3′
  • AhFAD2B-Allele-2 (wild-type allele, HEX™ dye): 5′-CAAACACTTCGTCGCGGTCG-3′
  • AhFAD2B-common (reverse primer): 5′-CCGCCACCACTCCAACACA-3′
  • Universal FRET Tail Sequences:
  • Allele-l Tail (FAM tail): 5′-GAAGGTGACCAAGTTCATGCT-3′
  • Allele-2 Tail (HEX tail): 5′-GAAGGTCGGAGTCAACGGATT-3′

A notable feature of the KASP primer design for peanut is the strategic placement of the common reverse primer's 3' end at an SNP position (Cytosine in AhFAD2A and Adenine in AhFAD2B at position 432 from the 5' end of the open reading frame). This allows for discrimination between the A and B subgenomes, effectively handling the homeologous nature of the genes and ensuring accurate and rapid genotype determination.30

The widespread adoption and successful application of KASP markers signify a broader trend towards more efficient, high-throughput, and cost-effective marker-assisted breeding strategies in peanut.2 This technology is instrumental in accelerating the development and commercial release of new high oleic cultivars, as evidenced by its use in major peanut-producing regions like China and India.2 The development of KASP markers represents a significant leap, enabling faster and more economical screening of large breeding populations, which directly translates to accelerated breeding cycles and more rapid development of improved high oleic cultivars to meet the growing demand for healthier edible oils.

4.3. Other Marker Technologies

In addition to CAPS and KASP, other PCR-based marker technologies have been developed and utilized for AhFAD2 mutation detection:

  • Allele-Specific PCR (AS-PCR): This method uses primers designed to specifically amplify only one allele (wild-type or mutant) based on a single nucleotide difference at the 3' end of the primer.8 It has been used to confirm true hybrids.1
  • Real-time PCR (qRT-PCR): This quantitative method allows for the rapid and precise identification of both wild-type and mutant alleles by measuring DNA amplification in real-time.3 It is particularly effective for the early elimination of undesirable non-high oleic acid lines in segregating populations, thereby streamlining breeding efforts.3
  • Simple Sequence Repeats (SSR) Markers: While not specific to high oleic acid, SSR markers are broadly used for evaluating molecular diversity in groundnut genotypes.1
  • Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) Markers: KASP is based on SNP markers.37 Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) have identified numerous SNPs associated with seed quality traits, including loci near FAD2A and FAD2B.4 The development of SNP array chips is essential for high-throughput genotyping in cultivated peanuts, particularly given their large genome size and limited genetic diversity.4

The availability of multiple PCR-based marker types provides flexibility for breeding programs, allowing them to choose the most suitable method based on their specific needs, resources, and desired throughput. The co-existence and continued use of various marker technologies imply that each method possesses unique strengths that make it optimal for different stages of a breeding program or for laboratories with varying levels of technological infrastructure. For instance, real-time PCR is valuable for early, rapid screening, while KASP excels in high-throughput applications.3 This indicates that breeders employ a strategic approach, selecting the marker technology that best fits the specific objective and scale of their genotyping efforts at different points in the breeding cycle.

5. Gene Sequence Information and Public Accessions

Access to publicly available genomic and gene sequence databases is paramount for molecular breeding, enabling researchers to elucidate the genetic underpinnings of traits, design novel molecular markers, and develop advanced gene-editing strategies.

5.1. Arachis hypogaea Genome Assemblies

The NCBI Assembly database serves as a comprehensive repository for numerous genome assemblies of Arachis hypogaea (peanut), encompassing both complete genome and chromosome-level assemblies.38 Examples of GenBank assembly accessions include GCA_039854485.1, GCA_039853315.1, GCA_003086295.3 (for the cultivar Tifrunner), and GCA_028451205.1 (for the cultivar BaileyII).38 These assemblies provide the essential genomic context required for identifying and characterizing genes such as AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B.4 For instance, the Tifrunner v1.0 assembly (PI 644011) was constructed using a combination of PacBio long reads and Illumina HiSeq reads, scaffolded with Hi-C technology, and spans approximately 2.54 Gb.21

Beyond NCBI, PeanutBase (dev.peanutbase.org) functions as a valuable specialized resource, offering extensive genomic data, gene search tools, and trait-specific information pertinent to Arachis species.5 The presence of these numerous genome assemblies and specific gene accessions on public platforms signifies a robust public data infrastructure. This accessibility is crucial for researchers to decode the genetic makeup of peanut 4 and to design new markers or gene-editing strategies, such as sgRNAs for CRISPR/Cas9.10

6. Application in Peanut Breeding Programs

The precise identification of specific AhFAD2 mutations and the subsequent development of robust molecular markers have profoundly transformed peanut breeding strategies for enhancing oleic acid content.

6.1. Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) and Marker-Assisted Backcrossing (MABC)

Molecular markers are routinely integrated into marker-assisted selection (MAS) protocols to screen for known ahFAD2 mutations.7 This approach significantly expedites breeding cycles by enabling the rapid identification of progeny carrying the desired high oleic acid alleles at early developmental stages, allowing for the efficient elimination of undesirable non-high oleic acid lines from segregating populations.3

Marker-Assisted Backcrossing (MABC) is a particularly crucial strategy employed to introgress the high oleic trait from donor parents into elite, yet low-oleic, recipient cultivars.1 This method is designed to ensure the recovery of the recurrent parent's desirable agronomic traits, such as high pod yield, optimal seed size, and disease resistance, while simultaneously incorporating the high oleic characteristic.1 This approach addresses the challenge of combining specific quality traits with overall agronomic performance.

Successful implementations of these molecular breeding strategies are evident in various peanut improvement programs:

  • High oleic lines have been successfully developed through crosses between elite cultivars (e.g., NC7) and advanced high oleic lines (HOG).1
  • The ahFAD2A and ahFAD2B mutant alleles originating from 'SunOleic 95R' have been effectively introgressed into popular Indian peanut cultivars like GG-7 and TKG19A, resulting in advanced lines with significantly increased oleic acid content (78–80%).2
  • In India, the first marker-assisted high oleic peanut varieties, Girnar 4 and Girnar 5, were released for commercial cultivation in 2020. These varieties boast an oleic acid content exceeding 78% and an O/L ratio of 17, demonstrating the tangible impact of MAS on cultivar development.2
  • Marker-assisted backcross selection has yielded high oleic acid lines (e.g., 24 lines from BC4F5 and BC5F5 populations) that exhibit morphological and agronomic traits comparable to their recurrent parents, with genetic background recovery rates ranging from 79.49% to 92.31%.24

6.2. Impact on Cultivar Development and Market Demands

The integration of molecular markers has significantly reduced the time required for the development and release of new high oleic cultivars.36 This acceleration is vital for meeting the increasing global demand for healthier and more stable peanut oil products.3 Molecular breeding further enables the pyramiding of the high oleic trait with other critical characteristics, such as resistance to foliar diseases (e.g., leaf spot and rust) and nematodes.1 This allows for the creation of advanced lines that combine superior oil quality with robust agronomic performance, addressing multiple breeding objectives simultaneously.1 Furthermore, genetic markers play a crucial role in quality control, facilitating the detection of seed mixtures and heterozygotes, thereby ensuring genetic purity in seed production.7

6.3. Challenges and Considerations

While the benefits of molecular breeding for high oleic acid are substantial, certain challenges and considerations must be addressed:

  • Pleiotropic Effects: A critical observation is that while fad2 mutations are essential for increasing oleic acid content, they have been noted to negatively affect peanut growth and development. This can manifest as dwarfing and a reduction in pod and seed size.6 This presents a significant trade-off that breeders must carefully manage. The goal is to develop varieties that not only possess high oleic acid but also maintain or improve crucial agronomic performance indicators such as yield and plant vigor.1 This necessitates a holistic breeding approach that balances oil quality with overall agronomic viability.
  • Narrow Genetic Base of Donors: The genetic background of existing high oleic acid donor parents is often limited, with many derived from small-seeded and late-maturing cultivars.24 This narrow genetic base can restrict the genetic diversity available for further breeding. Consequently, careful MABC strategies are essential not just for introgression but also for efficiently recovering the favorable genetic background and diverse traits of elite recurrent parents.
  • Environmental Influences: Intermittent failures of high oleic cultivars to consistently meet self-imposed oleic acid content thresholds have been observed.3 This suggests that environmental factors or other unidentified genetic elements may influence fatty acid composition.3 The overall genetics of oleic acid content can involve intricate quantitative trait loci (QTL) and multiple genes governing fatty acid biosynthesis beyond the primary AhFAD2 genes.3

Effective high oleic peanut breeding is a complex endeavor that extends beyond single-trait selection. It requires a comprehensive approach that balances the desired oil quality with crucial agronomic performance indicators (yield, plant vigor, disease resistance). Advanced breeding techniques like MABC are essential not only for trait introgression but also for minimizing undesirable pleiotropic effects and efficiently recovering the elite genetic background of recurrent parents, ultimately ensuring the commercial viability of new high oleic cultivars.

7. Conclusion

7.1. Recap of Critical Molecular Markers and Genetic Insights

The high oleic acid trait in peanut (Arachis hypogaea) is fundamentally governed by recessive, loss-of-function mutations within two homeologous genes, AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B. These genes encode Δ12 fatty acid desaturase enzymes, which are responsible for converting oleic acid to linoleic acid. The primary mutation in AhFAD2A is a G448A substitution, resulting in a D150N missense amino acid change. In AhFAD2B, a greater diversity of mutations has been identified, including the prevalent 441_442insA insertion (causing a frameshift and premature stop codon, notably from the F435 line), as well as other substitutions like G451T and C301G (leading to H101D), and various transposable element or MITE insertions. The necessity of inactivating both homeologous genes for high oleic acid expression underscores the inherent genetic redundancy in this allotetraploid species.

The advent and continuous refinement of molecular markers, particularly Cleaved Amplified Polymorphic Sequence (CAPS) and Kompetitive Allele Specific PCR (KASP) assays, have been instrumental in revolutionizing high oleic peanut breeding. CAPS markers leverage restriction enzyme polymorphisms to detect specific mutations, with established primer sequences and digestion patterns for key AhFAD2 alleles. KASP markers represent a significant leap forward, utilizing allele-specific primers and fluorescent dyes for high-throughput, cost-effective genotyping. The precise primer sequences for both AhFAD2A and AhFAD2B KASP assays are well-defined, enabling accurate and rapid detection of the underlying mutations. The availability of extensive Arachis hypogaea genome assemblies and specific AhFAD2 gene accession numbers in public databases such as NCBI and PeanutBase provides a critical foundational resource for ongoing molecular research and breeding innovation.

7.2. Future Prospects for Enhancing High Oleic Acid Trait in Peanut

The trajectory of peanut breeding for high oleic acid points towards continued advancements in molecular tools and integrated breeding strategies. The ongoing development and widespread application of high-throughput genotyping technologies like KASP, alongside emerging gene-editing technologies such as CRISPR/Cas9, will further enhance the efficiency and precision of high oleic peanut breeding. These tools facilitate targeted mutagenesis and the rapid introgression of desirable alleles into diverse genetic backgrounds, overcoming limitations imposed by narrow genetic diversity in existing donor pools.

Future research efforts will likely focus on several key areas:

  • Elucidating Complex Genetic Interactions: A deeper understanding of additional genetic factors or quantitative trait loci (QTLs) that influence fatty acid composition will be crucial. This will enable more precise fine-tuning of oleic acid levels and assist in mitigating any negative pleiotropic effects on essential agronomic traits such as yield and plant vigor.
  • Broadening Genetic Diversity: Exploring and utilizing novel AhFAD2 mutant alleles, whether naturally occurring or induced through advanced gene-editing techniques, will be vital for broadening the genetic base of high oleic germplasm. This diversification is essential for developing robust cultivars adaptable to various environments and agricultural systems.
  • Multi-Trait Breeding: The emphasis will increasingly be on developing comprehensive multi-trait breeding strategies. These strategies aim to combine high oleic acid content with other critical traits, including high yield potential, enhanced disease resistance, and improved stress tolerance. Such holistic improvement of peanut cultivars is necessary to ensure their sustainable production and meet evolving market demands for healthier, more stable, and agriculturally resilient peanut varieties globally.
