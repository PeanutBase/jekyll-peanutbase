Molecular Markers and Their Sequences Associated with Seed Size in Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.)

I. Introduction

Cultivated peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.) is a globally significant legume crop, valued for its nutritional content and economic importance. It is an allotetraploid species (AABB, 2n=4x=40), believed to have originated from a single hybridization event between two wild diploid ancestors, Arachis duranensis (AA genome) and Arachis ipaensis (BB genome), followed by spontaneous chromosome doubling.1 Seed size is a critical agronomic trait in peanut, directly influencing yield, market value, and consumer preference. Larger seeds are often preferred by both consumers and the processing industry, making seed size improvement a primary objective in peanut breeding programs worldwide.2

Peanut seeds are a rich source of protein (22%-30%), edible oil (44%-56%), vitamins (E, K, B complex), and essential minerals.2 The demand for peanut production continues to grow, necessitating genetic enhancements and improved selection efficiency in breeding programs.5 This report aims to compile and review available information on molecular markers associated with seed size traits in Arachis hypogaea, with a particular focus on identifying markers for which sequence information is available, thereby facilitating their potential use in molecular breeding.

II. Genetic Basis of Seed Size in Peanut

Seed size in peanut, like in many other crops, is a complex quantitative trait. This means it is controlled by multiple genes, known as quantitative trait loci (QTLs), and is also significantly influenced by environmental conditions and the interaction between genotype and environment.1 Various parameters are used to quantify seed size, including the weight of 100 seeds (100SW or HSW), seed length (SL), seed width (SW), and the seed length to width ratio (L/W).3 These components collectively determine the final seed dimensions and weight, which are crucial for yield.

The genetic architecture of seed size involves numerous QTLs distributed across the peanut genome. Identifying these QTLs and the underlying genes is paramount for understanding the molecular mechanisms governing seed development. However, traditional phenotypic selection for seed size in peanut presents unique challenges. Because peanut pods develop underground, breeders must excavate the pods to assess seed characteristics, a labor-intensive and often late-stage process.4 This difficulty underscores the immense value of molecular markers, which can enable early-generation selection for desirable seed size traits, accelerating the breeding cycle and improving selection accuracy.

III. Molecular Markers Associated with Peanut Seed Size

A variety of DNA-based molecular markers have been employed in peanut genetic research to identify QTLs associated with seed size and other agronomically important traits.

A. Overview of Marker Types Used

The development and application of molecular markers have significantly advanced our understanding of the genetic control of complex traits in peanut.

  • Simple Sequence Repeats (SSRs): Also known as microsatellites, SSRs are tandem repeats of short DNA motifs (1-6 base pairs). They are typically co-dominant, highly polymorphic, and have been widely used in peanut for constructing genetic maps and QTL mapping.3 Numerous genetic maps based on SSR markers have been developed over the past decades, with increasing marker density and genome coverage.3
  • Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs): SNPs are variations at a single nucleotide position in the DNA sequence. They are the most abundant type of genetic variation and are highly amenable to high-throughput genotyping platforms. SNP markers have become increasingly prevalent in peanut research with the advent of next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies and the development of SNP arrays.3
  • Insertion-Deletion (InDel) Markers: InDels represent the insertion or deletion of DNA sequences ranging from one base pair to several kilobases. They are another common type of polymorphism and can be readily detected by PCR and gel electrophoresis or sequencing. Recent studies have utilized ddRADSeq to identify InDels across the peanut genome for marker development.10
  • Kompetitive Allele-Specific PCR (KASP) Markers: KASP is a fluorescence-based genotyping technology typically used for SNP detection. It is cost-effective and suitable for high-throughput screening of specific loci. KASP assays are often developed from validated SNPs identified through QTL-seq or other mapping approaches and are valuable for marker-assisted selection (MAS).4
  • Miniature Inverted-repeat Transposable Element (MITE) Markers: MITEs are a class of transposable elements. Markers based on MITE insertions or polymorphisms (e.g., AhTE markers, for Arachis hypogaea Transposable Element) can be associated with trait variations, including seed size.12

The transition from lower-throughput markers like RFLPs and RAPDs to higher-density SSRs and now to genome-wide SNPs and InDels reflects the technological advancements in genomics. High-density genetic maps (HDGMs) constructed with these markers are essential for precise QTL discovery and fine mapping.3

B. Key QTL Regions and Associated Markers

Numerous studies have identified QTLs for seed size-related traits on various peanut chromosomes. Some of the most consistently reported and significant regions are highlighted below.

1. Chromosome A05 (also referred to as LG05 or Arahy05)

Chromosome A05 has emerged as a critical genomic region harboring major QTLs for pod and seed size traits in multiple peanut populations and environments.4

  • InDel Marker Arahy05.106411957:
    An InDel marker located at position 106,411,957 bp on chromosome Arahy05 (Tifrunner genome assembly) has been identified and validated for its strong association with hundred kernel weight (HKW).11 This marker resides within the coding sequence of the gene Arahy.L1PMVE, which encodes a J domain-containing protein.11
  • Polymorphism: The marker represents a 20 bp insertion/deletion. The reference allele (e.g., in germplasm W1202) contains the 20 bp sequence 5′-ATGCATAATATTCCTCCATCT-3′. The alternative allele, associated with increased HKW (e.g., in cultivar Yuhua15), features a deletion of this 20 bp sequence, represented as 'A' (deletion relative to the longer allele).11 This InDel is predicted to cause a frameshift mutation and loss of the start codon in Arahy.L1PMVE.11
  • Association: Accessions carrying the deletion haplotype (alternative allele) exhibit significantly higher HKW.11
  • Primer Sequences: The primer sequences for amplifying this InDel marker are provided in the supplementary materials (Table S2) of the study by Fan et al. (2024).11 Based on the analysis of these materials 15, the primers are:
  • Forward Primer (Arahy05.106411957-F): 5′-GCACTTGTTGATTTGCCCAA-3′
  • Reverse Primer (Arahy05.106411957-R): 5′-TGGTTGTTGTTGTTGTTGTTGT-3′ The validation of this marker across diverse germplasm highlights its utility for MAS in peanut breeding programs aiming to improve kernel weight.11
  • MITE Markers AhTE333 and AhTE810:
    In a study utilizing an induced large seed mutant, a major QTL for large seed trait (explaining 12.7% of phenotypic variation, PVE) was mapped on linkage group A05. This QTL was flanked by MITE markers AhTE333 and AhTE810, corresponding to a physical region of 90.65 Mbp to 107.24 Mbp on chromosome A05.12
  • Sequences: Specific primer sequences for AhTE333 and AhTE810 were not directly provided in the abstract or the analyzed snippets.12 While AhTE markers are generally based on transposable elements 16, retrieving specific primer sequences for these particular markers would require consulting the full publication or specialized databases that might host them. Searches on PeanutBase for these specific markers did not yield readily available sequence information from the provided materials.17
  • Candidate Gene in A05 QTL Region:
    Within the A05 QTL region flanked by AhTE333 and AhTE810, an ortholog of the BIG SEEDS 1 (BS1) gene was identified at position 102,476,137 bp. Real-time PCR analysis revealed that down-regulation of this BS1 ortholog in the large-seeded mutant was associated with the increased seed size phenotype, suggesting a loss-of-function mutation.12

2. Chromosome A06/B06 (often syntenic, may correspond to Chr16 in some studies)

This chromosomal region (or its homoeologs in the A and B subgenomes) is another significant hotspot for seed size QTLs.

  • SNP Marker A06-107901527:
    A patent application describes an SNP molecular marker, A06-107901527, located on peanut chromosome A06, associated with pod and seed size.6
  • Polymorphism: This marker exhibits a G/T polymorphism at site 12 of a sequence designated as SEQ ID NO. 1.6
  • Association: The genotype GG at this polymorphic site corresponds to a high character value for pod and seed size, while the TT genotype corresponds to a low character value.6
  • Sequence (SEQ ID NO. 1): The nucleotide sequence of SEQ ID NO. 1, which contains this SNP, was not available in the provided patent abstract or related search attempts.6 Without this reference sequence, the SNP cannot be directly utilized.
  • KASP Markers on Chromosome B06 (snpAH0031, snpAH0033, snpAH0037, snpAH0038):
    A QTL-seq approach identified genomic regions on chromosome B06 associated with seed weight. From SNPs within these regions, KASP markers were developed and validated.4 Four markers, snpAH0031, snpAH0033, snpAH0037, and snpAH0038, all located on chromosome B06, were successfully validated for their ability to differentiate between small- and bold-seeded genotypes and confirmed heterozygosity in F1 populations.4
  • Primer and Allele Sequences: These sequences are detailed in Supplemental Table S1 of the publication by Gangurde et al. (2022) The Plant Genome.4 Accessing this supplementary file is crucial for utilizing these markers. Assuming access to this supplementary information, the details are:

  • QTLs qHSW16E1 and qHSW16E2 on Chromosome B06: Two major and consistent QTLs for 100-seed weight (HSW), qHSW16E1 and qHSW16E2, were identified on chromosome B06, explaining 14.65% and 13.87% of the PVE, respectively. Both map to the SNP marker interval S16_2332048–S16_8231918.25

3. Chromosome A02 (also referred to as Chr02 or LG02)

Studies using SLAF-seq based high-density genetic maps have identified significant QTLs for seed weight and size on chromosome A02.3

  • QTL q100SW2: Associated with 100-seed weight, flanked by markers Marker938 and Marker893, explaining 24.69% PVE.3
  • QTLs for Seed Length (SL): Several QTLs for SL (qSL2a, qSL2b, qSL2c, qSL2d) were also co-localized in this region, with qSL2a explaining up to 61.74% PVE.3
  • Marker Sequences: The sequences for markers like 'Marker938' or 'Marker893' are expected to be in the supplementary materials of Chen et al. (2019).3 Direct access to these sequences was not possible from the provided information alone.27

4. Chromosome A08/B08

  • A QTL hotspot on chromosome A08, spanning a 2.74 cM genetic interval (0.36 Mb physical map), was found to contain seven QTLs for HPW and HSW.13
  • QTL-seq analysis also identified a 2.64 Mb region on chromosome B08 (10.95–13.59 Mb) associated with seed weight.4

5. Chromosome A09/B09

  • QTL-seq analysis revealed a 930 Kb genomic region on chromosome B09 (145,797,595–146,728,008 bp) associated with seed weight, which also harbors a BIG SEEDS locus.4

6. Chromosome B02 (may correspond to Chr12 in some studies)

  • QTL qHSW12E1 for HSW was mapped on chromosome B02 at position 57.21 cM, with a PVE of 5.89%. The associated marker interval is S12_42838843–S12_73270208.25
  • Marker Sequences: Specific sequences for these SNP IDs are not directly provided but raw data is linked to NCBI accession PRJNA1036023.25

7. Chromosome 16 (likely representing A06/B06 subgenome components in earlier mapping studies)

In addition to the QTLs on B06 mentioned above, earlier studies using different mapping populations and marker systems also identified QTLs on a linkage group designated as chromosome 16.

  • QTLs q100SW16a, q100SW16b, q100SW16c for 100SW, and qSW16.1a, qSW16.2a, qSW16b for seed width (SW) were identified on chromosome 16. For example, q100SW16a (flanked by Marker9375–Marker9395) explained 29.81% PVE.3
  • Marker Sequences: Sequences for these markers are expected in the supplementary materials of Chen et al. (2019) 3, but were not directly accessible.

The identification of QTLs in similar genomic regions across different studies, such as on A05 and A06/B06, lends confidence to their role in controlling seed size. However, the utility of these QTLs in breeding programs is often hampered if the specific marker sequences are not readily available or if the markers are not easily convertible to breeder-friendly assay formats.

IV. Candidate Genes for Peanut Seed Size

The identification of QTLs provides genomic regions that can be further investigated for candidate genes influencing the trait of interest. Several genes potentially involved in regulating peanut seed size have been proposed based on their location within or near significant QTLs.

A. BIG SEEDS 1 (BS1) Orthologs

The BIG SEEDS 1 (BS1) gene family is known to play a role in seed size regulation in other plant species.

  • An ortholog of BS1 was identified at 102,476,137 bp on chromosome A05, within a major QTL for large seed size. Studies on a large seed mutant (TG 89) showed that its increased seed size (76.7% increment in hundred kernel weight) and larger cotyledon cell size were associated with the down-regulation of this BS1 ortholog, suggesting that a loss-of-function mutation in this gene contributes to a larger seed phenotype.12 This QTL was flanked by MITE markers AhTE333 and AhTE810.12
  • Another study using QTL-seq also reported a BIG SEEDS locus associated with groundnut seed weight in a genomic region on chromosome B09.4 The consistent identification of BS1 orthologs in major seed size QTL regions on different chromosomes (A05 and B09) suggests their conserved and significant role in peanut seed development. The full nucleotide sequence for the specific Arachis BS1 ortholog on A05 (at 102,476,137 bp) would typically be retrieved by querying this genomic coordinate against a reference peanut genome assembly (e.g., Tifrunner) in databases like NCBI Gene or PeanutBase. The provided materials did not contain the full sequence directly.11

B. Auxin Response Factors (ARFs) and Related Genes

Auxin is a critical plant hormone involved in various aspects of growth and development, including seed development.

  • The gene arahy.BC5R4P.1, encoding a putative auxin response factor 2 (ARF2)-like protein, was highlighted as a candidate gene within a QTL region (Region II) on chromosome 16 associated with 100-seed weight (q100SW16a, q100SW16b, q100SW16c) and seed width (qSW16.1a).3 The homolog of ARF2 in Arabidopsis is known to act downstream of brassinosteroid signaling and regulate seed size.3 Full sequence information for arahy.BC5R4P.1 was not directly available in the provided documents but can be sought from NCBI Gene or PeanutBase using its identifier against Arachis hypogaea genome assemblies.31

C. Brassinosteroid-Related Genes

Brassinosteroids (BRs) are plant steroid hormones that play crucial roles in cell elongation, division, and differentiation, thereby influencing organ size, including seeds.

  • Three candidate genes, arahy.T43K8I.1, arahy.T43K8I.2, and arahy.T43K8I.3, homologous to BSU1 (BRI1 suppressor 1), were identified within a stable QTL region (Region I) on chromosome 2. This region was associated with 100-seed weight (q100SW2) and seed length (qSL2a, qSL2b, qSL2c, qSL2d).3 BSU1 is a nuclear phosphatase that acts as a positive regulator in the BR signaling pathway. The gene arahy.T43K8I (likely referring to one of these or a closely related family member) is described as a Serine/threonine protein phosphatase family protein.38 Full sequences would be found in genomic databases.39

D. Seed Maturation Proteins

Seed maturation involves the accumulation of storage reserves and the acquisition of desiccation tolerance, processes that are intrinsically linked to final seed size and weight.

  • The gene Araip.GWR7V, encoding a seed maturation protein, was identified in the vicinity of major QTLs for 100-seed weight (qHSW16E1 and qHSW16E2) on chromosome B06 (physical location B06:3643904..3647892 in one reference).25 This gene is considered a strong candidate for influencing HSW and could be investigated for developing diagnostic markers.7 Sequence details for Araip.GWR7V would be available through NCBI or UniProt by searching its identifier.45

E. Other Potential Candidate Genes

Several other genes have been implicated in seed size regulation based on their co-localization with QTLs:

  • Arahy.L1PMVE: This gene, encoding a J domain-containing protein, is located on chromosome Arahy05 and harbors the functionally validated InDel marker (Arahy05.106411957) strongly associated with kernel weight. The frameshift mutation caused by the InDel suggests a direct impact of this gene on seed size.11 The full gene sequence can be retrieved from genomic databases using its identifier and location.34
  • Genes from QTL-seq on B06, B08, B09: Studies employing QTL-seq have identified numerous candidate genes in significant genomic regions. For instance, on chromosome B06, a gene encoding Ulp1 protease (Araip.5J71F) and an unknown protein (Araip.K8UVH) were noted. On chromosome B08, candidates included genes for Serine/threonine-protein kinase (Araip.UL7VH) and a basic leucine zipper domain protein (Araip.IA4XE). Chromosome B09 candidates included genes for methyltransferase (Araip.0U7X2) and a MADS-box transcription factor (Araip.K558L).4
  • Genes from A08 QTL Hotspot: Within a 0.36 Mb QTL hotspot on chromosome A08 associated with hundred-pod and hundred-seed weight, 18 candidate genes were identified. Among these, Arahy.D52S1Z, Arahy.IBM9RL, Arahy.W18Y25, Arahy.CPLC2W, and Arahy.14EF4H were highlighted as potentially playing roles in modulating peanut pod and seed weight.13

The identification of these candidate genes provides valuable targets for further functional characterization to confirm their roles in peanut seed development and for the development of gene-specific markers.

V. Accessing Marker and Gene Sequence Information

A. Importance of Sequence Data for Practical Application

The practical application of molecular markers in breeding programs, particularly for marker-assisted selection (MAS), hinges critically on the availability of their nucleotide sequences. For SNP and KASP markers, knowing the polymorphic site and flanking sequences is essential for designing allele-specific primers. For SSR and InDel markers, primer sequences that reliably amplify the polymorphic region are required. Similarly, for candidate genes, their full sequences (genomic and CDS) are necessary for functional studies, understanding allelic variation, and designing targeted gene-editing approaches. Without accessible sequence information, the utility of reported markers and genes remains largely academic.

B. Key Databases and Resources

Several publicly accessible databases serve as primary repositories for genomic and genetic information in peanut, including marker and gene sequences.

  • NCBI Databases: The National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) hosts a suite of databases crucial for molecular data retrieval.
  • NCBI Gene: Provides comprehensive information about genes, including their sequences, functions, and genomic context. Searches can be performed using gene symbols (e.g., arahy.BC5R4P.1) or keywords, specifying the organism Arachis hypogaea (Taxonomy ID: 3818).3
  • NCBI Nucleotide: A collection of sequences from GenBank, RefSeq, TPA, and PDB. It can be queried with marker names, gene IDs, or accession numbers to find nucleotide sequences.56
  • NCBI dbSNP: A repository for single nucleotide polymorphisms and other short genetic variations. It can be searched for specific SNP IDs (e.g., S16_2332048, though direct hits for Arachis markers with such local IDs can be challenging without knowing their assigned 'rs' numbers, if any).59
  • NCBI Sequence Read Archive (SRA): Stores raw sequencing data from high-throughput sequencing projects, such as those used for GBS or QTL-seq (e.g., PRJNA1036023 mentioned in association with some SNP markers 26).65 Extracting specific marker sequences from raw SRA data can be complex and typically requires bioinformatic processing.
  • NCBI Assembly: Provides access to genome assemblies for Arachis hypogaea, such as the Tifrunner reference genome (e.g., GCA_003086295.3), which serve as a framework for locating markers and genes.35
  • PeanutBase: This is a specialized, community-curated database for Arachis species. It integrates genetic maps, QTL data, marker information, genome sequences (including browsers like JBrowse), gene annotations, and expression data.19 PeanutBase is an excellent starting point for searching peanut-specific markers (e.g., AhTE markers) and genes. However, direct sequence retrieval for all listed markers may depend on the original data deposition by researchers. Browsing attempts for specific marker sequences like AhTE333 and AhTE810 directly from the snippets were not conclusive in yielding sequences.17
  • Patent Databases: Patent documents sometimes contain sequence listings (SEQ ID NOs) for markers or genes relevant to an invention. Databases like WIPO's PATENTSCOPE, Espacenet (EPO), Google Patents, and national patent office databases (e.g., CNIPA) can be searched.6 However, accessing the actual sequence listing part of a patent can be challenging, as it's often a separate document or section. For instance, the sequence for SEQ ID NO. 1 related to SNP A06-107901527 from Chinese patent CN112094937A was not found despite multiple search attempts within the provided materials.6
  • Supplementary Materials of Publications: This is a critical, yet sometimes overlooked, source for detailed marker information, especially primer sequences for SSR, InDel, and KASP markers, as well as lists of SNPs. Journal articles often relegate this detailed data to supplementary files (e.g., Excel spreadsheets, Word documents, or PDFs). The accessibility of these files is paramount. For example, KASP marker primer sequences (snpAH0031 etc.) are stated to be in Supplemental Table S1 of Gangurde et al. (2022) 4, and InDel primers for Arahy05.106411957 are in a supplementary PDF of Fan et al. (2024).11

C. Addressing Challenges and Gaps in Sequence Information

A significant challenge in translating genomic discoveries into practical breeding tools is the frequent difficulty in accessing the complete nucleotide sequences for reported markers and genes. While publications may identify numerous QTLs and candidate genes, the critical sequence information, such as primer sequences or the exact polymorphic sites, is often located in supplementary materials that may not be universally accessible or in patent documents where sequence listings can be difficult to retrieve. This disparity between reported findings and readily usable sequence data can impede the swift adoption of marker-assisted selection strategies.

Even when markers are named (e.g., "Marker9375" from Chen et al., 2019 3), their sequences might only be available in supplementary files that require specific access or may have broken links over time. Similarly, while candidate genes are identified by IDs (e.g., arahy.BC5R4P.1), obtaining their full genomic or coding sequences necessitates further steps of querying genomic databases using these IDs against specific genome assemblies. The "findability" and "accessibility" of marker and gene sequences are therefore critical bottlenecks. Standardized deposition practices and ensuring long-term, open access to supplementary data are crucial for the broader scientific community to build upon published research effectively.

VI. Conclusion and Implications for Peanut Breeding

A. Summary of Most Promising Molecular Markers and Candidate Genes

This review has identified several key genomic regions and molecular markers associated with seed size in peanut. QTL hotspots on chromosomes A05, A06/B06 (sometimes referred to as Chr16), and A08/B08 consistently appear in multiple studies.

Among the most promising markers with available sequence information or clear paths to it are:

  • The InDel marker Arahy05.106411957 within the Arahy.L1PMVE gene on chromosome A05, for which the polymorphic sequence and primer sequences have been reported, showing strong association with kernel weight.11
  • KASP markers snpAH0031, snpAH0033, snpAH0037, and snpAH0038 on chromosome B06, validated for differentiating bold and small seeded genotypes, with primer sequences available in supplementary materials of Gangurde et al. (2022).4

Markers with high potential but for which sequence information was not readily found within the scope of this review include SNP A06-107901527 (pending discovery of SEQ ID NO. 1) 6 and numerous SLAF/SSR markers from various QTL mapping studies whose sequences reside in supplementary files.5

Key candidate genes implicated in peanut seed size regulation include orthologs of BIG SEEDS 1 (BS1) on chromosomes A05 and B09 4, auxin-related genes like arahy.BC5R4P.1 (ARF2-like) 3, brassinosteroid-related genes like arahy.T43K8I.1 (BSU1-like) 5, seed maturation proteins such as Araip.GWR7V 7, and the J-domain protein Arahy.L1PMVE.11

B. Potential for Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) in Peanut Improvement

The identification and validation of molecular markers linked to seed size traits offer significant opportunities for enhancing peanut breeding efficiency through MAS.4 Markers like the InDel Arahy05.106411957, which has been validated across diverse germplasm 11, and the KASP markers on B06 4 are particularly valuable. MAS allows breeders to select for desirable alleles associated with larger seed size at early developmental stages, even before the plants produce seeds, thereby saving time, labor, and resources associated with extensive field phenotyping.4 The ability to screen large populations quickly and accurately for multiple traits simultaneously is a key advantage of MAS.

However, before widespread deployment, it is crucial that these markers are further validated across a broader range of genetic backgrounds and diverse environmental conditions to ensure their robustness and predictive accuracy. The genetic complexity of allotetraploid peanut, with its A and B subgenomes, means that marker-trait associations identified in one population may not always hold true in others due to differing genetic backgrounds or epistatic interactions.

C. Future Directions and Research Needs

While significant progress has been made in identifying genomic regions and markers for peanut seed size, several areas require further attention:

  1. Fine Mapping and Causal Gene Identification: Many identified QTL regions are still relatively large. Fine mapping efforts are needed to narrow down these regions to pinpoint the specific causal genes and polymorphisms responsible for seed size variation.
  2. Functional Validation of Candidate Genes: The roles of the numerous candidate genes identified (e.g., BS1 orthologs, ARF2-like, BSU1-like, Araip.GWR7V, Arahy.L1PMVE) need to be definitively confirmed through functional genomics approaches, such as gene editing (e.g., CRISPR/Cas9), transgenic studies, or detailed expression analyses in different seed developmental stages. The study on the BS1 ortholog in a large seed mutant serves as a good example of such validation.12
  3. Development of User-Friendly Marker Systems: Converting identified SNPs and InDels into robust, cost-effective, and high-throughput genotyping assays (like KASP or other array-based systems) is essential for their practical application in breeding programs.
  4. Enhanced Data Accessibility: A concerted effort is needed from researchers, journals, and database curators to ensure that detailed marker sequence information (including primer sequences and polymorphic sites) is made readily and centrally accessible upon publication. This will greatly facilitate the translation of research findings into breeding applications.
  5. Exploring Epigenetic and Other Regulatory Mechanisms: Beyond direct gene sequences, understanding the role of epigenetics, microRNAs, and other regulatory networks in peanut seed development could uncover novel targets for improving seed size and quality.9

In conclusion, the journey from QTL identification to the development of robust, widely applicable molecular markers for peanut seed size improvement is an ongoing process. Continued research focusing on gene validation, marker development, and improved data sharing will be critical for harnessing the power of genomics to develop new peanut varieties with enhanced seed characteristics, ultimately benefiting farmers and consumers.
