Molecular Markers and Genomic Regions Associated with Flowering Time and Maturity in Cultivated Peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.)

1. Introduction

1.1. Significance of Flowering Time and Time-to-Maturation (TTM) in Peanut

Cultivated peanut (Arachis hypogaea L.) is a globally significant legume crop, valued for its high oil and protein content. The timing of flowering initiation and the subsequent duration required to reach physiological maturity, often collectively referred to as Time-to-Maturation (TTM), are paramount traits influencing the crop's adaptability, productivity, and quality. Peanut exhibits a unique geocarpic reproductive strategy, producing aerial flowers but developing fruits (pods) underground after fertilization and the elongation of a specialized structure called the peg or gynophore. The duration from planting to maturity dictates the suitability of a cultivar for specific agro-ecological zones, particularly those defined by limited growing seasons or the prevalence of end-of-season stresses like drought. Early maturation (e.g., 90-120 days post-planting) is a critical adaptive feature in regions with short rainy seasons, such as West Africa and parts of India, enabling drought avoidance. Conversely, longer-maturing varieties may have higher yield potential in environments with extended favorable conditions.

The TTM significantly impacts peanut yield and economic value. Optimizing the match between a cultivar's maturity period and the available growing season is essential for maximizing pod development, seed filling, and overall harvest index (HI) – the ratio of pod weight to total biomass. Consequently, understanding and manipulating TTM is a primary objective in peanut breeding programs worldwide, aiming to develop cultivars with appropriate maturity durations for diverse production systems. The economic viability of peanut cultivation relies heavily on aligning planting and harvesting schedules with the specific maturation characteristics of the chosen cultivar to achieve optimal yield and grade.

It is important to distinguish between "flowering time" (often measured as days to first flower or days to 50% flowering) and the broader concept of "Time-to-Maturation" (TTM) or "Maturity Index" (MI), which integrates the entire period from planting to harvest readiness, encompassing flowering, pegging, and pod/seed development. While flowering time is a component of TTM, the overall maturation period is influenced by the duration of subsequent developmental stages as well. Some genetic factors might specifically affect flowering initiation, while others could influence the pace of pod development or overall plant senescence, thereby impacting TTM/MI. Therefore, markers associated with TTM or MI, while highly relevant to the overall goal of managing crop duration, may not exclusively control flowering initiation itself but rather the integrated process leading to maturity. Recognizing this distinction is crucial when interpreting the function and application of identified molecular markers.

1.2. Genetic Complexity and Environmental Influences

Flowering time and TTM in peanut are recognized as complex quantitative traits, meaning their expression is governed by the interplay of multiple genes (polygenic control) and is significantly modulated by environmental conditions. Factors such as photoperiod, temperature, soil moisture, altitude, and latitude can exert considerable influence on the phenology of peanut development. This complexity presents challenges for breeders attempting to select for specific maturity types consistently across different locations and years. Some studies have reported low heritability estimates for TTM, suggesting a strong environmental component relative to the genetic contribution in those specific populations and environments. For instance, the broad-sense heritability for MI in a Virginia-type RIL population was estimated at 0.39, indicating moderate-to-low but significant genetic influence. However, other studies investigating related yield and quality traits in diverse germplasm have reported high broad-sense heritability (e.g., 88-98%), and the identification of major Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs) for various traits, including TTM/MI, demonstrates that a substantial and selectable genetic component underlies these traits. The observed heritability can depend heavily on the genetic diversity within the population studied, the environmental conditions, and the precision of phenotyping.

The genetic architecture of peanut is further complicated by its evolutionary history. Cultivated peanut is a relatively recent allotetraploid (AABB genome, 2n = 4x = 40), originating from a single hybridization event between two wild diploid species, Arachis duranensis (A genome) and Arachis ipaënsis (B genome), followed by spontaneous chromosome doubling approximately 9,000-10,000 years ago. This recent polyploidization event created a significant genetic bottleneck, resulting in a remarkably narrow genetic base within the cultivated species compared to its wild relatives. This limited genetic diversity historically hampered efforts in genetic mapping and marker development, as finding sufficient polymorphism between parental lines was often difficult. Furthermore, the presence of two similar, yet distinct, subgenomes (A and B) complicates genetic analyses, particularly the accurate identification and scoring of molecular markers like Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs), requiring specialized bioinformatic approaches to distinguish between homoeologous variants (variants at corresponding locations on the A and B subgenomes) and true allelic variation. Significant genotype × environment (GxE) interactions are also commonly observed for peanut traits, implying that the effects of specific genes or QTLs on flowering time or TTM might vary depending on the environmental context, further complicating breeding efforts. Understanding these inherent biological complexities – the quantitative nature of the trait, environmental sensitivity, allotetraploidy, and narrow genetic base – is essential for interpreting genetic studies and devising effective breeding strategies.

1.3. Role of Molecular Markers and Genomic Approaches

Despite the challenges, significant progress has been made in dissecting the genetic basis of flowering time, TTM, and other important agronomic traits in peanut through the application of molecular markers and advanced genomic techniques. QTL mapping in biparental populations (such as Recombinant Inbred Lines - RILs) and Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) in diverse germplasm panels have emerged as powerful strategies for identifying genomic regions associated with phenotypic variation. These approaches allow researchers to link specific DNA sequence variations, such as Simple Sequence Repeats (SSRs), SNPs, or Insertions/Deletions (InDels), to differences in traits like flowering time or maturity.

The identification of robust molecular markers tightly linked to genes controlling TTM is highly valuable for practical breeding applications. Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) utilizes these DNA markers to predict the phenotypic potential of individual plants early in the breeding cycle, often at the seedling stage. This allows breeders to select desirable genotypes more efficiently, accurately, and rapidly compared to relying solely on traditional phenotypic selection, which can be time-consuming, labor-intensive, and confounded by environmental effects. The development of peanut genomics has seen a transition from using relatively low-density marker systems like SSRs to employing high-throughput SNP genotyping arrays and whole-genome resequencing approaches. These advancements provide much greater marker density and genome coverage, significantly enhancing the resolution and power of genetic mapping studies, even within the context of peanut's low polymorphism and polyploid complexity. This report synthesizes current knowledge on molecular markers and genomic regions associated with flowering time and TTM in Arachis hypogaea, drawing upon findings from QTL mapping and GWAS studies, and discusses their potential application in peanut improvement.

2. Genetic Architecture of Flowering Time in Peanut

2.1. Subspecies Differentiation

The cultivated peanut species, A. hypogaea, is primarily classified into two major subspecies: ssp. hypogaea and ssp. fastigiata. These subspecies represent distinct evolutionary lineages that likely arose during the processes of domestication and diversification in South America. They exhibit contrasting characteristics, particularly in growth habit, flowering patterns, and maturity duration.

  • A. hypogaea ssp. fastigiata: This subspecies includes the Spanish and Valencia market types. Genotypes within fastigiata are generally characterized by an erect growth habit, determinate shoot formation (the main stem terminates in an inflorescence), sequential flowering (presence of flowers on the main stem, often denoted as MSF+), and typically early fruit maturation (often 90-120 days).
  • A. hypogaea ssp. hypogaea: This subspecies encompasses the Virginia and Runner market types. Genotypes within hypogaea usually display a spreading or bunch growth habit, indeterminate shoot formation (the main stem remains vegetative), an alternative flowering pattern (absence of flowers on the main stem, MSF-), and are generally later maturing (often 130-170 days).

This fundamental divergence in flowering pattern (sequential vs. alternative) and associated maturity level represents a major axis of genetic variation for phenological traits in peanut. The genetic control of these subspecies-defining traits, particularly the presence or absence of mainstem flowering, has been a subject of significant research interest.

2.2. Inheritance Patterns

Flowering time and TTM are generally considered quantitative traits in peanut, controlled by multiple genes (polygenes), each potentially contributing a small to moderate effect on the overall phenotype. This polygenic control, combined with the significant environmental influence discussed earlier, contributes to the continuous variation observed for these traits in segregating populations and germplasm collections.

While some studies reported low heritability for TTM, suggesting challenges in selection, numerous QTL mapping and GWAS investigations have successfully identified specific genomic regions (QTLs) that exert a measurable influence on flowering time, TTM, or related traits. The identification of major QTLs, defined as those explaining a substantial portion of the phenotypic variance (e.g., >10% PVE), has been reported not only for TTM but also for other complex traits in peanut, such as pod and seed characteristics, shelling percentage, and disease resistance. This indicates that despite the polygenic nature, genes with larger effects do exist and can be targeted for selection. For example, the locus controlling mainstem flowering (MSF) acts like a major gene differentiating the subspecies. Studies investigating the inheritance of related traits, like growth habit (lateral branch angle - LBA), suggested control by multiple genes, as segregation ratios in an F2 population did not fit a simple single-gene model.

It is plausible, and indeed likely, that the specific genetic architecture underlying flowering time variation differs depending on the genetic backgrounds being compared. Studies involving crosses between the two subspecies (hypogaea x fastigiata) are likely to capture the effects of major genes differentiating them, such as the MSF locus. In contrast, studies focusing on variation within a single subspecies, such as the analysis of TTM variation among Virginia-type cultivars, may primarily identify QTLs representing modifiers of maturity within that specific genetic background. These intra-subspecific QTLs might have smaller individual effects compared to the major subspecies-differentiating loci but are nonetheless crucial for fine-tuning maturity within a given market class. Therefore, comparing QTL results across different studies requires careful consideration of the parental materials and the type of genetic variation being investigated (inter- vs. intra-subspecific).

3. Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs) Associated with Flowering Time / Time-to-Maturation

3.1. Overview of QTL Mapping Studies

QTL mapping has been extensively applied in peanut genetics and breeding to identify genomic regions controlling economically important traits. Despite the inherent challenges posed by the crop's narrow genetic base and allotetraploid genome, numerous studies have successfully mapped QTLs for a wide range of characteristics, including yield components, oil and protein quality, drought tolerance, disease resistance, and pod/seed morphology. Several studies have specifically focused on identifying QTLs associated with flowering time, TTM, or related phenological traits.

These studies typically employ structured populations, most commonly RIL populations derived from crosses between parents exhibiting contrasting phenotypes for the trait of interest. Genetic linkage maps are constructed using molecular markers (historically SSRs, more recently high-density SNPs), and phenotypic data collected across multiple environments (locations and/or years) are analyzed to detect statistical associations between marker genotypes and trait variation. Early studies were sometimes limited by the low density and polymorphism of available markers, but the advent of high-throughput SNP genotyping has enabled the construction of much more saturated maps, improving the precision and power of QTL detection.

3.2. Key QTLs for TTM / Maturity Index (MI)

A significant study investigating the genetic control of TTM within the late-maturing Virginia market type (ssp. hypogaea) utilized an RIL population derived from a cross between cultivars 'Hanoch' and 'Harari', which differed in TTM by 12-15 days. Phenotyping for Maturity Index (MI) over two years (2018 and 2019) and genotyping with a high-density SNP array led to the identification of four QTL regions for MI. Notably, two of these regions showed consistent effects across both years, representing robust QTLs for TTM in this genetic background.1

  • qMIA04a,b (Chromosome A04): This consistent QTL was detected on chromosome A04 in both 2018 (qMIA04a) and 2019 (qMIA04b).
  • Location: Chromosome A04. The physical interval identified spanned approximately from 117.6 Mbp to 125.6 Mbp in 2018 and 118.6 Mbp to 125.6 Mbp in 2019, based on the flanking SNP markers mapped to the Tifrunner reference genome. This corresponds to a genomic region of roughly 6.9 to 8 Mbp.1
  • Flanking Markers: The interval was defined by SNP markers AX-176802283_A04 and AX-176815499_A04 (2018), and AX-176819644_A04 and AX-176815499_A04 (2019).1
  • Statistical Significance: The QTL exhibited Logarithm of Odds (LOD) scores of 5.33 (2018) and 6.45 (2019), explaining 9.9% and 11.9% of the phenotypic variance (PVE) for MI in the respective years.1
  • qMIB03a,b (Chromosome B03): A second consistent QTL was identified on chromosome B03 in both years (qMIB03a in 2018, qMIB03b in 2019).
  • Location: Chromosome B03. The physical interval was mapped from approximately 2.8 Mbp to 4.6 Mbp (2018) and 2.8 Mbp to 5.7 Mbp (2019), representing a region of about 1.8 to 2.9 Mbp.1
  • Flanking Markers: Defined by SNP markers AX-176807311_B03 and AX-176806413_B03 (2018), and AX-176807311_B03 and AX-176801237_B03 (2019).1
  • Statistical Significance: This QTL showed LOD scores of 5.0 (2018) and 5.35 (2019), explaining 9.3% and 9.9% of the PVE for MI, respectively.1

Interestingly, the study also found that QTLs for Harvest Index (HI) co-localized with the MI QTLs on chromosomes B03, as well as on B05 and B06. This co-localization suggests either pleiotropy (where the same gene influences both traits) or tight linkage of separate genes controlling MI and HI within these genomic regions. This finding underscores the intricate relationship between plant development duration and yield accumulation efficiency.

3.3. QTL for Mainstem Flowering (MSF) / Flowering Pattern

As previously discussed, the presence or absence of flowers on the main stem (MSF) is a key morphological trait distinguishing ssp. fastigiata (MSF+) from ssp. hypogaea (MSF-). A study specifically aimed at mapping this trait utilized SNP array genotyping in populations segregating for MSF.2

  • Location: The major locus controlling MSF was mapped to the distal end of chromosome B02.2 Initial mapping placed it within a 3.1 Mbp interval (between markers AX-176791582_B02 and AX-176803063_B02 based on the genetic map), which was refined using the physical Tifrunner genome sequence to a 1.7 Mbp segment defined by markers AX-176805026_B02 and AX-176803063_B02.2
  • Significance: This locus represents a major genetic determinant responsible for the differing flowering patterns observed between the two primary subspecies and likely played a significant role during peanut domestication and diversification. Its large effect makes it a prime target for understanding the evolution of flowering habits in Arachis.

3.4. Other Potential QTLs/Associations

Other studies have also reported QTLs or associations related to flowering time, although sometimes with less detail provided in the available summaries. Wang et al. (2020), cited in, identified three main-effect QTLs for the initial flowering date using an RIL population, but specific genomic locations and marker details were not provided in the reference snippet.

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) offer a complementary approach to QTL mapping, leveraging historical recombination in diverse germplasm panels to identify marker-trait associations. While the provided materials do not contain detailed results from a GWAS specifically focused on flowering time, reference is made to a study that identified 259 SNP loci significantly associated with flowering time in a large panel of peanut cultivars, predicting seven candidate genes in associated regions.3 This highlights the utility of GWAS for exploring the genetic basis of this trait. Furthermore, GWAS studies on related traits have identified significant loci: pleiotropic QTLs on chromosome 16 (Chr16) were associated with multiple yield components, while loci on Chr08 were linked to protein and oil content. A GWAS for drought tolerance identified significant QTLs on multiple chromosomes (A03, A05, A06, A07, A08, B04, B05, B06, B09, B10) associated with pod weight and count under drought stress. Although not directly for flowering time under optimal conditions, these loci influencing yield and stress response could potentially interact with maturity pathways. A study on Groundnut Rosette Disease (GRD) resistance identified multiple significant SNPs on chromosome A04, a chromosome also harboring a major TTM QTL (qMIA04). While these GRD SNPs are linked to a different trait, their location warrants investigation for potential linkage or pleiotropic effects related to plant development or stress response influencing maturity.

4. Molecular Markers (SNPs, SSRs, InDels) for Peanut Flowering Time

4.1. Overview of Marker Types Used

The exploration of peanut genetics has utilized various types of molecular markers over time. Early studies employed techniques like Random Amplified Polymorphic DNA (RAPD), Amplified Fragment Length Polymorphism (AFLP), and Inter Simple Sequence Repeat (ISSR) markers. However, these markers often suffered from limitations in reproducibility or genome coverage.

Simple Sequence Repeat (SSR) markers, also known as microsatellites, became more prevalent due to their co-dominant nature and relative abundance. Thousands of SSRs were developed from SSR-enriched genomic libraries and Expressed Sequence Tags (ESTs) in peanut. SSRs were instrumental in constructing early genetic linkage maps and assessing genetic diversity. However, a major limitation encountered in cultivated peanut was the low level of polymorphism detected with SSRs; estimates suggested less than 15% of developed SSRs showed polymorphism between cultivated peanut lines, with an even smaller fraction being mapped, largely due to the crop's narrow genetic base resulting from the polyploidization bottleneck. For instance, a genetic map used for QTL mapping of gynophore-pod structure traits utilized 830 polymorphic SSR markers.

More recently, Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) have become the dominant marker type in peanut genomics, driven by advancements in sequencing and genotyping technologies. High-density SNP arrays, such as the Affymetrix Axiom_Arachis array series (e.g., Axiom_Arachis2 used in), allow for the simultaneous genotyping of tens of thousands of SNPs across the genome.1 Furthermore, whole-genome resequencing (WGRS) of parental lines or entire populations enables the discovery of millions of SNPs and other variants. These high-density SNP datasets have facilitated the construction of highly saturated genetic maps (e.g., 1833 SNPs, 5120 SNPs) and powered GWAS studies, significantly improving the resolution for locating trait-associated loci.

Insertion-Deletion (InDel) markers represent another valuable source of polymorphism. InDels arise from the insertion or deletion of one or more nucleotides at a specific genomic location. Studies have suggested that InDels might exhibit higher levels of polymorphism than SSRs in some crops, and they can be readily detected. Research in peanut has involved the development of gene-specific InDel markers, with some showing utility in diversity analysis and potential for trait association. A functionally significant 1492 bp deletion within the AhTFL1 gene serves as a powerful marker for the MSF trait.2

4.2. Specific Markers Associated with Key QTLs

Based on the QTL mapping studies discussed, specific markers have been identified within or flanking the key genomic regions associated with TTM/MI and MSF.

  • Markers for qMIA04a,b (TTM QTL on A04): The consistent QTL for Maturity Index on chromosome A04, identified in the Hanoch x Harari RIL population, was flanked by SNP markers derived from the Axiom_Arachis array. Key markers defining this region include:
  • AX-176802283_A04
  • AX-176819644_A04
  • AX-176815499_A04 These markers delimit the ~117.6-125.6 Mbp region associated with TTM variation in Virginia-type peanuts.1
  • Markers for qMIB03a,b (TTM QTL on B03): Similarly, the consistent TTM QTL on chromosome B03 in the same population was associated with the following Axiom array SNP markers:
  • AX-176807311_B03
  • AX-176806413_B03
  • AX-176801237_B03 These markers define the ~2.8-5.7 Mbp interval linked to MI variation.1
  • Markers for MSF Locus (Flowering Pattern on B02): The major locus controlling mainstem flowering at the end of chromosome B02 was mapped using SNP markers and further validated with a gene-specific InDel:
  • AX-176805026_B02 (SNP)
  • AX-176803063_B02 (SNP)
  • AX-176791582_B02 (SNP)
  • A 1492 bp deletion within the AhTFL1 gene (InDel). This deletion serves as a highly diagnostic marker for the presence/absence of mainstem flowering, showing a very strong association with the trait.2
  • Markers from GWAS and Other Studies: While specific sequences are often not provided directly for GWAS hits in summary reports, marker identifiers are usually given. For example, GWAS for GRD resistance listed SNP IDs like AX-147219775, AX-147219783, etc., on chromosome A04. Markers developed from resequencing data for KASP assays are often named based on their genomic position, such as A12.4097252 (Chromosome A12, position 4,097,252 bp), which was developed for bacterial wilt resistance. Such position-based nomenclature, tied to a reference genome assembly (like Tifrunner 2), provides a standardized way to identify and locate markers discovered through sequencing, overcoming the variability seen in older marker naming conventions (e.g., SSR prefixes like 'Ah-', 'TC-', 'Seq-' seen in). Careful tracking of marker IDs and their corresponding genomic coordinates is essential when comparing results across different studies and platforms.

4.3. Marker Sequences and Accessibility

Obtaining the exact DNA sequences for molecular markers can vary depending on the marker type and origin. For SNP markers identified using commercial arrays (e.g., Affymetrix Axiom "AX-" markers), the specific sequence information surrounding the SNP might be proprietary or require access to manufacturer-provided annotation files. However, the genomic coordinates (chromosome and base pair position) provided 1 allow researchers to locate the marker position within publicly available reference genome sequences (e.g., Tifrunner genome available via PeanutBase or NCBI). This enables retrieval of the flanking sequence context necessary for designing alternative assays or further investigation.

For markers developed from WGRS, such as SNPs targeted for KASP assays or specific InDels like the AhTFL1 deletion 2, the sequence variation itself and the flanking sequences required for primer/probe design are typically provided in the supplementary materials of the original publication or can be readily extracted from the reference genome using the reported coordinates.

For SSR markers, the original publications usually report the forward and reverse primer sequences needed to amplify the microsatellite region. Databases like PeanutBase 4 serve as central repositories aiming to consolidate marker information, including sequences and genomic locations where available. Researchers seeking to utilize specific markers should consult the primary literature and relevant databases for the most accurate and complete sequence information.

4.4. Statistical Validation

The reliability of a molecular marker for research or breeding purposes depends on the statistical strength of its association with the trait of interest. For QTLs identified through linkage mapping, the LOD score is a key statistic, indicating the likelihood of linkage versus chance association. A commonly accepted threshold for declaring a significant QTL is a LOD score of 2.5 or 3.0, although this can vary. The TTM QTLs qMIA04a,b and qMIB03a,b exhibited peak LOD scores ranging from 5.0 to 6.45, well above typical significance thresholds, indicating strong statistical support for their association with Maturity Index.1 The MSF locus on B02 also showed high LOD scores, particularly when the functional AhTFL1 deletion marker was used, confirming its major role.2

The proportion of phenotypic variance explained (PVE) by a QTL provides an estimate of its effect size. The consistent TTM QTLs qMIA04 and qMIB03 explained approximately 9-12% of the variation in MI each.1 While statistically significant, these represent moderate effects, consistent with the quantitative nature of TTM within the Virginia market class. In contrast, major QTLs for other traits, like rust resistance or pod weight, have been reported with PVE values exceeding 20%, 30%, or even 80% in some cases, indicating genes with larger impacts on those specific traits. The MSF locus, differentiating subspecies, effectively explains a very large proportion of the variance for the mainstem flowering trait.

For GWAS, significance is typically assessed using p-values, often adjusted for multiple testing (e.g., using False Discovery Rate - FDR). Stringent p-value thresholds (e.g., p<10−5 or 10−6) or corresponding -log10(p) values (e.g., > 4.5 or 5.0) are used to declare significant associations. PVE estimates can also be calculated for significant GWAS loci.

5. Candidate Genes Implicated in Flowering Time Regulation

Identifying the specific genes underlying QTLs is a crucial step towards understanding the biological mechanisms controlling traits and developing highly accurate markers. While QTL mapping points to genomic regions, these regions can contain numerous genes. Further fine-mapping or candidate gene approaches are needed to pinpoint the causal variants.

5.1. Genes within QTL Regions

  • AhTFL1 (Terminal Flower 1 homolog): The most compelling candidate gene identified in the context of peanut flowering pattern is AhTFL1, located within the major MSF locus on chromosome B02.2 Homologs of TFL1 in model plants like Arabidopsis typically act as floral repressors, maintaining indeterminate growth at the shoot apex and delaying flowering. The presence or absence of mainstem flowering in peanut subspecies aligns conceptually with differential regulation or function of a TFL1-like gene. The identification of a significant deletion within AhTFL1 that strongly correlates with the MSF phenotype provides strong evidence for its causal role in differentiating the alternative (MSF-, hypogaea) and sequential (MSF+, fastigiata) flowering patterns.2 Sequence analysis suggested gain/loss events of this deletion contributed to the evolution of MSF in Arachis. This represents a successful example of moving from a mapped locus to a highly plausible candidate gene with functional implications.
  • Genes in TTM QTL regions (A04, B03): For the consistent TTM QTLs identified on chromosomes A04 (qMIA04a,b) and B03 (qMIB03a,b) 1, the associated genomic intervals are relatively large (~1.8-8 Mbp). Such regions inevitably contain hundreds of genes. The source publications identifying these QTLs did not report specific candidate genes within these intervals responsible for the observed effects on Maturity Index. Identifying the causal genes within qMIA04 and qMIB03 will require further research, potentially involving fine-mapping with higher marker density in larger populations (as demonstrated for a bacterial wilt resistance QTL, qBWA12, which was narrowed down to a 216.7 kb region containing candidate genes), comparative genomics, and transcriptomic analyses (like RNA-seq) to identify differentially expressed genes between lines with contrasting alleles. While not directly related to flowering, studies on other traits have successfully identified candidate genes within QTL regions, such as seed maturation protein-coding genes for hundred-seed weight (HSW), highlighting the feasibility of this approach in peanut.

5.2. Gene Families Involved in Flowering Pathways

Beyond genes located within specific QTL intervals, studies investigating key gene families known to regulate flowering time in other plant species provide additional candidates for controlling this trait in peanut.

  • PEBP Family (FT/TFL1 homologs): The Phosphatidylethanolamine-Binding Protein (PEBP) family includes critical regulators of the transition to flowering, notably the FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) and TERMINAL FLOWER 1 (TFL1) subfamilies. FT homologs generally act as floral promoters (florigen), moving from leaves to the shoot apex to induce flowering, while TFL1 homologs act as repressors, maintaining vegetative growth. A study systematically identified PEBP family members in the peanut genome and analyzed their expression patterns.5 Several specific peanut PEBP genes were highlighted as potentially key regulators responding to photoperiod and temperature cues, suggesting their involvement in controlling flowering time: ArahyM2THPA, ArahyEM6VH3, Arahy4GAQ4U, ArahyIZ8FG5, ArahyG6F3P2, ArahyLUT2QN, ArahyDYRS20, and ArahyBBG51B.5 While AhTFL1 (likely Arahy.TFL1b based on) is implicated in the MSF locus, the roles and genomic locations of these other PEBP candidates relative to the TTM QTLs on A04 and B03 require further investigation.
  • AhCOL Family (CONSTANS-like): The CONSTANS (CO) gene and its homologs (CONSTANS-like, COL) play a central role in the photoperiodic flowering pathway, typically acting upstream of FT to regulate its expression in response to day length. An analysis of the peanut genome identified 18 AhCOL genes.3 Expression profiling revealed that many AhCOL genes are expressed in leaves and flowers. Notably, AhCOL4, AhCOL8, AhCOL13, and AhCOL14 were implicated in light response and potentially pod development.3 Furthermore, protein interaction studies suggested AhCOL1 and AhCOL3 might play roles in flowering regulation.3 These AhCOL genes represent potential upstream regulators of flowering time in peanut, although their specific contributions and linkage to known QTLs need to be established.

Pinpointing causal genes within QTL intervals, especially large ones identified in initial mapping studies, remains a significant challenge in polyploid crops like peanut. The successful identification of AhTFL1 as the likely gene underlying the major MSF locus demonstrates feasibility.2 However, for quantitative traits influenced by multiple loci with smaller effects, like TTM within a subspecies, integrating fine-mapping, transcriptomics, and candidate gene approaches based on known flowering pathways (involving PEBP, COL, and other families) will be necessary to unravel the precise molecular mechanisms.

6. Bioinformatic Resources and Databases

Access to centralized and well-curated bioinformatic resources is essential for modern plant genetics research and breeding. Several key databases provide valuable information for peanut researchers studying flowering time and other traits.

6.1. PeanutBase

PeanutBase (peanutbase.org) stands as the primary, community-driven online resource dedicated to peanut genomics and genetics. It serves as a vital hub integrating diverse data types relevant to the study of flowering time and other traits. Key features include 4:

  • Genome Sequences: Access to reference genome assemblies for cultivated peanut (e.g., A. hypogaea cv. Tifrunner 2) and its diploid progenitors (A. duranensis, A. ipaënsis).
  • Genetic Maps and Markers: Collections of genetic linkage maps and information on molecular markers (SSRs, SNPs, etc.), including their sequences and positions where available.
  • QTL and GWAS Data: Dedicated tools and browsers (e.g., Trait Search Tool, QTL/GWAS viewers) allow users to search for and visualize QTLs and GWAS hits associated with specific traits, including potentially flowering time or TTM.4
  • Gene Information: Gene models, functional annotations, gene family classifications, and orthology relationships with other species.
  • Trait Descriptions: Information on various peanut traits and phenotypic characteristics.

PeanutBase provides the necessary framework for researchers to locate markers like those listed in Table 2 within the genomic context, retrieve flanking sequences, explore genes within QTL regions, and access curated trait association data.4

6.2. NCBI Databases

The National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) hosts fundamental biological databases widely used by the scientific community.

  • PubMed: Provides access to the vast majority of peer-reviewed scientific literature, including the publications cited throughout this report (e.g.,).
  • GenBank: A comprehensive public database of nucleotide sequences. Researchers can deposit and retrieve sequences for genes, markers, and entire genomes. Marker sequences or flanking regions, if made public by the original researchers, can often be found here.

6.3. Other Resources

Leveraging these bioinformatic resources is crucial for accessing marker sequences, validating genomic locations, identifying candidate genes within QTL intervals, and staying updated on the latest research findings related to peanut flowering time genetics.

7. Application in Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) and Future Prospects

7.1. MAS Potential

A primary motivation for identifying QTLs and associated molecular markers is their potential application in Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) to enhance breeding efficiency. By using DNA markers linked to desirable alleles for flowering time or TTM, breeders can screen large populations of plants at an early stage, selecting individuals with the desired genetic makeup without needing to wait for laborious and environmentally sensitive field phenotyping. This can significantly shorten breeding cycles and increase the accuracy of selection, particularly for complex traits like TTM.

The SNP markers identified for the TTM QTLs (qMIA04, qMIB03) and the MSF locus (especially the AhTFL1 deletion) hold promise for MAS.1 SNPs are particularly amenable to high-throughput, cost-effective genotyping platforms like Kompetitive Allele-Specific PCR (KASP). Successful development and validation of KASP markers for yield, quality, and disease resistance traits in peanut have already been demonstrated, providing a clear pathway for converting the identified flowering time/TTM markers into practical breeding tools. For instance, the KASP marker A12.4097252, developed for a major bacterial wilt resistance QTL, was validated in a large collection of breeding lines, proving its utility for MAS. Similar validation efforts would be necessary for the TTM markers.

7.2. Challenges and Future Directions

Despite the potential, several challenges need to be addressed for the effective implementation of MAS for flowering time/TTM in peanut.

  • Marker Validation: QTL effects can sometimes be specific to the population in which they were identified or influenced by the environment (GxE interactions). Therefore, markers linked to qMIA04 and qMIB03 need rigorous validation across diverse genetic backgrounds and multiple environments relevant to target breeding programs before widespread deployment. The consistency of these QTLs across two years in the original study is encouraging, but broader validation is crucial. In contrast, the AhTFL1 deletion marker for MSF, being directly linked to a likely causal gene with a large, qualitative effect differentiating subspecies, is expected to be more robust and immediately applicable in breeding scenarios involving crosses between fastigiata and hypogaea types.2
  • Fine-Mapping and Gene Identification: The TTM QTL intervals on A04 and B03 are still relatively large.1 Fine-mapping these regions to narrow down the location of the causal genes would increase marker accuracy (by reducing the chance of recombination between the marker and the gene) and provide deeper biological insights.
  • Polyploid Complexity: Continued development of analytical tools and strategies specifically designed for polyploid genomes will aid in more accurate marker discovery and genotyping.

Future research should focus on:

  1. Fine-mapping the consistent TTM QTLs (qMIA04, qMIB03) to identify candidate genes and develop markers closer to the causal variants.
  2. Functional characterization of candidate genes, including AhTFL1 and members of the PEBP and COL families, to confirm their roles in regulating peanut flowering and maturity pathways.
  3. Expanded GWAS using larger, more diverse germplasm panels and higher-density marker data to uncover additional loci contributing to flowering time variation across the species.
  4. Validation of promising markers (like those flanking qMIA04 and qMIB03) in relevant breeding populations and target environments.
  5. Integration of marker information into genomic selection (GS) models, which can simultaneously account for numerous small-effect loci across the genome, potentially offering advantages over traditional MAS for highly complex traits like TTM.

8. Conclusion

8.1. Synthesis of Findings

Significant strides have been made in elucidating the genetic basis of flowering time and time-to-maturation in cultivated peanut, leveraging advances in molecular marker technology and genomic resources. QTL mapping and GWAS studies have successfully identified key genomic regions associated with these critical agronomic traits. Notably, consistent QTLs for TTM/Maturity Index have been mapped to chromosomes A04 (qMIA04a,b) and B03 (qMIB03a,b) in Virginia-type peanuts, explaining roughly 9-12% of the phenotypic variance each.1 Furthermore, a major locus controlling the mainstem flowering pattern (MSF), a key trait differentiating ssp. fastigiata and ssp. hypogaea, has been pinpointed to the distal end of chromosome B02.2

Specific molecular markers, primarily SNPs derived from array-based genotyping or resequencing, have been linked to these loci.1 The identification of the AhTFL1 gene, harboring a functional deletion within the MSF locus, represents a significant breakthrough in connecting a mapped QTL to a causal gene.2 Additionally, studies exploring key flowering gene families have identified potential candidates from the PEBP (FT/TFL1-like) and COL (CONSTANS-like) families that likely contribute to the regulation of flowering time in response to environmental cues in peanut.3 Bioinformatic resources like PeanutBase provide essential platforms for accessing and integrating this rapidly growing body of genetic and genomic information.4

8.2. Implications for Research and Breeding

The identified QTLs, markers, and candidate genes offer valuable resources for both fundamental research and applied peanut breeding. They provide insights into the genetic architecture of adaptation in peanut, the molecular pathways controlling flowering and maturity, and the evolutionary consequences of domestication and polyploidization.

From a breeding perspective, the molecular markers associated with qMIA04, qMIB03, and the MSF locus hold considerable potential for use in Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS). The AhTFL1 deletion marker appears particularly robust for manipulating flowering patterns in inter-subspecific crosses. The TTM markers require further validation across diverse germplasm and environments but offer promising avenues for accelerating the development of cultivars with optimized maturity durations tailored to specific production regions and cropping systems. Continued research focused on fine-mapping existing QTLs, identifying causal genes, discovering novel loci through GWAS, and validating markers for breeding utility will further enhance our ability to understand and manipulate flowering time and maturity in peanut, ultimately contributing to improved crop productivity and resilience.
